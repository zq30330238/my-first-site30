Feng Shui Basics (风水基础)

The ancient art of harmonizing buildings with the natural and cosmic environment.

Feng shui (风水), literally "wind and water," is the ancient Chinese system of geomancy that governs the relationship between human structures and the natural environment. For over three thousand years, feng shui principles have guided the siting, orientation, and design of Chinese buildings, from imperial palaces and capital cities to humble farmhouses and graves. Understanding feng shui is essential to understanding Chinese architecture, because feng shui is not merely a superstition but a comprehensive system of environmental design that encodes practical wisdom about site planning, climate response, and human psychology.

Core Principles

The fundamental goal of feng shui is to harmonize human structures with the flow of qi (气), the vital energy that animates the cosmos. Qi is influenced by the configuration of landforms, the presence of water, the orientation of buildings, and the arrangement of interior spaces. Good feng shui is achieved when a building is positioned to receive favorable qi while being protected from negative sha qi (煞气) — the "killing energy" generated by straight lines, sharp angles, and hostile landforms.

The classic feng shui site is protected by hills at the rear, open to water at the front, and flanked by lower hills on both sides. This configuration, known as the "armchair" formation, creates a sheltered microclimate that protects the building from cold north winds while allowing sunlight and favorable breezes to reach the site. The presence of water — a river, lake, or pond — is considered essential, as water is the carrier of qi and brings prosperity to the inhabitants. This ideal site configuration, rooted in empirical observation of environmental conditions, has guided Chinese site selection for millennia.

"Feng shui is not a religion or a superstition in the conventional sense. It is a practical science of site analysis that evolved over centuries of observation and experiment. The principles of feng shui — protection from wind, access to water, orientation to the sun — are the same principles that any sensible builder would follow, codified into a system of symbols and metaphors."

— Dr. Stephen Skinner, Feng Shui Scholar and Author

The Feng Shui Compass

The feng shui compass (罗盘, luo pan) is the primary tool of the feng shui practitioner. Unlike a standard magnetic compass, the luo pan is a complex instrument that incorporates multiple rings of information about directions, celestial influences, and elemental forces. The practitioner uses the luo pan to determine the precise orientation of a building and to identify favorable and unfavorable directions for various building elements. The compass readings are interpreted in relation to the site's landforms, the occupants' birth dates, and the building's function.

The Eight Trigrams (八卦, ba gua) of the I Ching form the theoretical basis of feng shui analysis. Each trigram represents a direction, a natural element, a family member, and a set of qualities. The interaction between the trigrams determines the flow of qi within a building and the suitability of different spaces for different activities. The Bagua map (八卦图) is used to analyze existing buildings, identifying areas that correspond to wealth, health, relationships, career, and other aspects of life that can be enhanced through feng shui adjustments.

Feng Shui in Architecture

Feng shui principles have profoundly influenced Chinese architectural design at every scale. At the urban scale, the planning of capital cities like Beijing and Xi'an followed feng shui principles, with the main axis aligned north-south, the imperial palace facing south, and protective hills to the north. At the building scale, the orientation of the main entrance, the placement of the kitchen and bathroom, the location of the staircase, and even the colors used in decoration are all subject to feng shui considerations.

Many features of traditional Chinese architecture that we admire for their aesthetic qualities originated as practical feng shui devices. The screen wall at the entrance, which we discussed in a previous article, was originally a feng shui device to block the straight-line entry of negative energy. The curved roof eaves serve, among their many functions, to deflect harmful energies downward and outward. The courtyard itself is a feng shui feature, creating a space where positive qi can accumulate and circulate for the benefit of the household’s inhabitants.
