The Blazer: From Rowing Club to Boardroom Essential

The blazer is the most versatile piece of tailoring in the modern wardrobe. More formal than a sports coat but less formal than a suit jacket, the blazer occupies a unique space in men's and women's fashion. Its history spans nearly two centuries and encompasses naval uniforms, school dress codes, and the feminist reclamation of power dressing. Today, the blazer is the ultimate transitional garment, equally at home in the boardroom and at a weekend brunch.

Naval Origins

The blazer's story begins in 1837 aboard HMS Blazer, a British Royal Navy ship. The captain, seeking a way to smarten his crew's appearance for a visit from Queen Victoria, ordered double-breasted blue serge jackets with brass buttons for his men. The result was so impressive that the style was adopted by other ships and eventually by civilian rowing clubs at Oxford and Cambridge. The name 'blazer' came from the blazing red jackets worn by the Lady Margaret Boat Club at Cambridge, which were described as 'blazing' in color.

From School Uniform to Fashion Staple

The blazer was adopted by British public schools in the late nineteenth century as part of their uniform. Each school developed its own distinctive blazer, with specific colors, badges, and trim that identified the wearer's institution. This practice continues today and has spread to schools around the world. Beyond school uniforms, the blazer became a staple of men's casual wardrobes, worn with flannel trousers for a relaxed but put-together look that remains a classic of British style.

Women and the Blazer

Women's adoption of the blazer was a significant moment in fashion history. In the late nineteenth century, women began wearing blazers for sporting activities like cycling and tennis, challenging the restrictive corsetry of the era. Coco Chanel's iconic tweed jacket, introduced in the 1920s, can be seen as a feminine reinterpretation of the blazer. The 1980s power-dressing movement saw women adopt oversized, heavily structured blazers with exaggerated shoulders as a way of claiming authority in the corporate world.

The blazer is tailoring's greatest transformer. Throw it over jeans and you're dressed. Throw it over a dress and you're sophisticated. Throw it over a suit and you're authoritative. One garment, three completely different looks, zero effort.

The Classic Navy Blazer

The navy blazer with gold or silver buttons is the most iconic version of the garment. Its deep blue color is universally flattering and pairs with virtually any other color or pattern. The gold buttons add a touch of formality without requiring a full suit. The classic navy blazer is typically single-breasted with two or three buttons, though double-breasted versions remain popular for a more formal look. It can be worn with gray trousers for business, with chinos for casual occasions, or with jeans for a relaxed weekend look.

Modern Blazer Styles

Contemporary blazers come in countless variations. Summer blazers in linen or cotton are lighter and more relaxed. Evening blazers in velvet or silk add luxury to formal occasions. Unstructured blazers, which lack the heavy canvas interfacing of traditional tailoring, offer a softer, more comfortable fit that works for casual settings. The blazer has also become a key item in sustainable fashion, as it is one of the most frequently found items in vintage stores and can be easily altered to fit a new wearer.

Did You Know?

A well-made traditional blazer is held together by hundreds of hand stitches that are completely invisible from the outside. The canvas chest piece, which gives the jacket its shape, is pad-stitched by hand in a process that takes a skilled tailor several hours.
