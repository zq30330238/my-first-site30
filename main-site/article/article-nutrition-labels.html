How to Read Nutrition Labels Like a Pro

May 21, 2026 7 min read

Walking through the grocery aisle can feel overwhelming when every package boasts health claims. But the real story is on the Nutrition Facts label. Understanding this small box is a powerful skill that pays off for healthier eating. In fact, a study published in the Journal of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics found that people who regularly read nutrition labels consume significantly fewer calories, less fat, and more fiber. Let’s break it down so you can read labels like a pro.

Why Nutrition Labels Matter

The Nutrition Facts label is a tool designed to help you make informed choices. It provides standardized information about serving sizes, calories, nutrients, and ingredients. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) updated the label in 2016 to reflect new scientific evidence, making it easier to understand. Yet many people skip this step. According to a survey by the International Food Information Council, only about 60% of Americans look at the label at all, and far fewer know how to use it effectively. By mastering label reading, you can better manage weight, reduce your risk of chronic diseases, and ensure you’re getting the nutrients your body needs.

Start with Serving Size

The first and most critical piece of information is the serving size. All the numbers that follow are based on this amount. If you eat double the serving size, you double the calories, fat, and other nutrients. A common mistake is assuming a package contains one serving when it often contains two or more. For example, a 20-ounce soda is typically 2.5 servings, even though most people drink the whole bottle. The FDA now requires serving sizes to reflect what people actually eat, but it’s still up to you to check. Pro tip: Look at the number of servings per container. If you plan to eat more than one serving, you’ll need to do some quick math.

A 2021 study found that 69% of consumers said they check the serving size first, but only 34% correctly identified the number of servings in a multi-serving package. — FDA Behavioral Research

Calories and Macronutrients

Calories get a lot of attention, and for good reason—they provide a measure of energy. But calories alone don’t tell the full story. Pay attention to the macronutrients: fat, carbohydrates, and protein. Look at the types of fat: limit saturated fat and trans fat. The American Heart Association recommends that saturated fat should be less than 7% of daily calories. For a 2,000-calorie diet, that’s about 16 grams. Also, check for added sugars. The updated label now includes a separate line for added sugars, which was a meaningful change. The Dietary Guidelines for Americans advise limiting added sugars to less than 10% of daily calories. That’s about 50 grams for a 2,000-calorie diet. Protein is generally a positive—aim for 20-30 grams per meal for satiety.

The % Daily Value Explained

The % Daily Value (%DV) shows how much a nutrient in a serving contributes to a daily diet based on a 2,000-calorie reference. This is a quick way to see if a food is high or low in a nutrient. As a rule of thumb, 5% DV or less is low, and 20% DV or more is high. Use this to choose foods high in nutrients you want more of (fiber, vitamin D, calcium, iron, potassium) and low in nutrients you want less of (saturated fat, sodium, added sugars). The %DV can be misleading if you eat more or fewer than 2,000 calories, but it remains a helpful benchmark. For example, a food with 20% DV of calcium per serving is a good source; one with 5% is not.

According to the FDA, only about 30% of consumers understand how to use the %DV correctly. Yet those who do are more likely to select nutrient-dense foods.

Ingredients List Matters

The ingredients list is often overlooked but is incredibly revealing. Ingredients are listed in descending order by weight, so the first few ingredients make up the bulk of the product. If sugar, refined flour, or unhealthy oils appear early, the product is likely less nutritious. Look for whole foods like whole wheat, oats, nuts, or seeds as first ingredients. Also, watch out for hidden sources of sugar, such as high-fructose corn syrup, cane sugar, agave, or honey. The shorter the list, generally the better—but a long list isn’t necessarily bad if it includes recognizable, wholesome items. Be aware of food additives and preservatives, but don’t fear them all; many are safe. Focus on the bigger picture of the whole food.

Putting It All Together

Reading nutrition labels takes practice, but it becomes second nature quickly. Start by checking the serving size and compare it to what you actually eat. Next, look at the calories but prioritize the nutrient quality. Use the %DV to quickly identify high and low nutrients. Finally, scan the ingredients list to see if the food is built on whole, minimally processed ingredients. Remember, no single food makes or breaks a healthy diet—it’s the overall pattern that counts.
