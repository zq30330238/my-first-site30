Common Pet Health Issues Every Owner Should Recognize

May 21, 2026 8 min read

As a pet owner, your furry companion’s health is likely one of your top priorities. Yet many common health issues go unnoticed until they become serious. Recognizing early signs can make a world of difference in treatment outcomes and your pet’s quality of life. In this article, we’ll walk through the most prevalent pet health problems, backed by expert insights and data, so you can stay one step ahead.

Dental Disease: The Silent Epidemic

Dental disease is the most common health issue in pets, affecting over 80% of dogs and 70% of cats by age three. It begins with plaque buildup, leading to gingivitis, periodontal disease, and even tooth loss. Symptoms include bad breath, yellow or brown teeth, drooling, and reluctance to eat.

“Periodontal disease doesn’t just affect the mouth—it can harm the heart, kidneys, and liver,” warns Dr. Sarah Mitchell, a board-certified veterinary dentist. Studies show that pets with severe dental disease have a 20% higher risk of developing systemic infections.

Prevention is straightforward: brush your pet’s teeth daily with pet-safe toothpaste, provide dental chews, and schedule annual professional cleanings. Catching dental issues early can save your pet from unnecessary pain and costly procedures.

Obesity: A Growing Concern

Obesity is a major epidemic among pets. According to the Association for Pet Obesity Prevention, an estimated 60% of cats and 56% of dogs in the U.S. are overweight or obese. Excess weight contributes to diabetes, arthritis, heart disease, and a shortened lifespan.

“Just a few extra pounds can cut a dog’s life expectancy by up to two years,” says Dr. Emily Carter, a veterinary nutritionist. “Even a 10% weight reduction can significantly improve mobility and reduce pain.”

Signs of obesity include difficulty feeling ribs, a sagging belly, and lack of energy. To manage weight, measure food portions, limit treats, and aim for at least 30 minutes of daily exercise. Your vet can help set a target weight and recommend a diet plan.

Ear Infections: More Common Than You Think

Ear infections are especially prevalent in dogs with floppy ears, such as Cocker Spaniels and Basset Hounds. Up to 20% of dogs will experience at least one ear infection in their lifetime. Cats are less prone, but infections still occur, often due to mites or allergies.

“Chronic ear infections can lead to hearing loss and balance issues,” notes Dr. Alex Rivera, a veterinary dermatologist. “Prompt treatment is crucial—never use cotton swabs inside the ear canal.”

Symptoms include head shaking, scratching, redness, odor, and discharge. Prevention involves keeping ears dry, cleaning them weekly with a vet-recommended solution, and addressing underlying allergies. If you notice signs, visit your vet for proper diagnosis and medication.

Skin Allergies and Dermatitis

Skin allergies affect roughly 10–15% of dogs and 5–10% of cats. Common triggers include food ingredients, pollens, dust mites, and flea saliva. The resulting dermatitis causes intense itching, hair loss, red skin, and secondary infections.

“Allergic dermatitis is one of the top reasons for vet visits,” says Dr. Karen Lee, a veterinary allergy specialist. “Identifying the trigger through elimination diets or allergy testing can dramatically improve your pet’s comfort.”

Preventive measures include using flea control year-round, bathing with hypoallergenic shampoo, and avoiding known allergens. If your pet is constantly scratching, consult your vet for antihistamines, immunotherapy, or dietary changes.

Arthritis and Joint Pain in Senior Pets

As pets age, arthritis becomes a common issue. Approximately 20% of dogs over one year old have osteoarthritis, and the prevalence increases with age. Cats are also affected, though they often hide signs well. Joint pain leads to stiffness, limping, reluctance to jump, and behavioral changes.

“Early intervention can slow arthritis progression,” advises Dr. Michael Tan, a veterinary orthopedic surgeon. “Weight management, joint supplements like glucosamine, and low-impact exercise are key.”

Provide soft bedding, ramps for furniture, and consider therapies like acupuncture or hydrotherapy. Pain medications and joint injections can help if prescribed by your vet. Regular check-ups are essential for older pets to manage pain and maintain mobility.

Gastrointestinal Upsets: When to Worry

Vomiting and diarrhea are common in pets, but frequent episodes may indicate a chronic condition. Food sensitivities, infections, parasites, or inflammatory bowel disease can be culprits. Studies show that up to 15% of dogs suffer from chronic GI issues.

“If vomiting or diarrhea lasts more than 24 hours, or if you see blood, lethargy, or appetite loss, seek veterinary care immediately,” emphasizes Dr. Laura Jensen, a veterinary internal medicine specialist.

To reduce GI upset, introduce new foods gradually, avoid table scraps, and keep your pet hydrated. Probiotics can help maintain gut health. Your vet can run tests to identify underlying causes and recommend appropriate diets or medications.

Recognizing these common health issues early can significantly improve your pet’s well-being. Stay observant, schedule regular vet visits, and maintain a proactive care routine.
