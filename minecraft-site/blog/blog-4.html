Nether Highway Construction: Efficient Travel in Minecraft

April 4, 2025 · 6 min read

Why Build a Nether Highway

Traveling long distances in the Overworld can waste time and resources. The Nether operates on a 1:8 scale, meaning every block you move in the Nether equals eight blocks in the Overworld. This makes Nether highways the fastest way to connect bases, farms, or biomes. A well-built highway cuts travel time by a factor of eight, letting you cover thousands of blocks in minutes.

Nether highways also bypass hazardous Overworld terrain like oceans, mountains, and dense forests. Once you link your portals through a highway, you can teleport between distant locations instantly, then ride the ice path for rapid point-to-point movement.

Pro tip: Place soul sand on the floor of the tunnel to prevent mobs from spawning, and use jack-o-lanterns for lighting to avoid fire risk. Soul sand slows mobs but not players on blue ice, keeping the path clean and safe.

Materials and Preparation

Gather the following before you start digging. For the tunnel, you need pickaxes with high durability, preferably diamond or netherite, and enchanted with Efficiency and Unbreaking. Bring stacks of blocks for marking and filling gaps--cobblestone or netherrack works fine since you will replace the floor with ice.

The key material is blue ice. It offers the lowest friction of any block, allowing boats to reach maximum speed. Nine packed ice craft into one blue ice, so you need large quantities. Alternatively, use packed ice if blue ice is scarce--it still moves boats fast, just slightly slower. For safety, bring enough torches, jack-o-lanterns, or glowstone to light the entire tunnel. Light level above 7 prevents hostile mob spawning. Use jack-o-lanterns or glowstone placed on the ceiling to avoid interfering with the ice path.

Also pack blocks for staircases, slabs, and fence gates. You can use slabs on the ceiling to prevent ghast spawns, and fence gates on the sides to keep boats on track while allowing you to dismount.

Building the Tunnel and Path

First, locate your starting Nether portal. Determine the coordinates of both portals, then calculate the difference. From your first portal, dig a three-block-high, two-block-wide tunnel in a straight line toward the second portal. Use the F3 screen to maintain a constant Y-level (around Y=120 to avoid lava lakes and terrain, but any consistent elevation works). Maintain a 2-block height to allow a boat to pass without hitting your head.

Once the tunnel reaches the target area, create a junction box approximately 10 blocks wide around the second portal coordinate. Build the portal and link it to the Overworld. Now lay the ice path: place blue ice in a single row along the center of the tunnel floor. Optionally, place slabs on the ceiling every third block to prevent ghast spawns. Use fence gates or buttons on the walls every 10 blocks to ensure the boat stays centered. Avoid water or lava near the path--they break boat speed or destroy items.

Test the route by mounting a boat and riding from end to end. If you hit a wall, adjust the tunnel width or add fence gates. Finally, light every block surface to keep the tunnel spawn-proof. For junctions, build ice paths branching off to different Outposts or farms, and label them using signs or colored banners.

A well-maintained Nether highway pays for itself quickly. You can ride from one end to the other in under a minute for a 2000-block Overworld distance. With blue ice, you achieve the fastest travel possible without the risk of elytra flight or netherite armor durability loss. Use this method for server travel, trading routes, or quick access to far-off biomes like the Deep Dark or Ancient City.
