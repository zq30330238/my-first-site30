Starting an LLC: Step-by-Step Legal Guide

May 21, 2026 8 min read

Disclaimer: The content on this site is for informational and educational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. Always consult a licensed attorney for legal matters specific to your situation.

Understanding the Legal Foundation of an LLC

The Limited Liability Company (LLC) has become one of the most popular business structures for entrepreneurs, freelancers, and small business owners. Its appeal lies in the powerful combination of personal asset protection—similar to a corporation—with the tax flexibility and operational simplicity of a partnership. By forming an LLC, you create a separate legal entity that shields your personal assets (house, car, savings) from business debts and lawsuits. However, this shield is only as strong as the steps you take to properly form and maintain the LLC. A single misstep in the formation process—such as incorrectly filing Articles of Organization or failing to maintain a registered agent—can leave you personally exposed. This guide walks you through each legal step required to start an LLC, from naming your business to drafting an operating agreement.

Step 1: Choose a Legally Compliant Business Name

Your LLC’s name must comply with state-specific legal requirements. Every state requires that the name be distinguishable from existing business entities registered in that state. Additionally, the name must include "Limited Liability Company" or an abbreviation such as "LLC" or "L.L.C." You cannot use words that could confuse your business with a government agency (e.g., "Treasury," "FBI," "Secret Service") unless you obtain special permission. Some states also restrict terms like "Bank," "Insurance," or "University" without meeting licensing requirements. Before you settle on a name, conduct a free name search on your Secretary of State’s website. It is also wise to check federal trademark databases to avoid infringing on another company’s trademark rights—which could later force you to rebrand. Many entrepreneurs reserve their desired name for 30 to 120 days while preparing the rest of the paperwork.

Step 2: Appoint a Registered Agent

Every LLC must have a registered agent with a physical street address (not a P.O. Box) in the state of formation. The agent is the official point of contact for service of process (lawsuits), government correspondence, and annual report reminders. The agent must be available during normal business hours to accept legal documents. While you can serve as your own registered agent, this means you must be present at your registered address (often your home or office) during business hours. If you change addresses frequently or want legal privacy—since the agent’s name and address become public record—consider hiring a commercial registered agent service. Annual costs typically range from $50 to $300, which is a small price for peace of mind. Many states require the agent’s consent, so ensure the person or entity agrees to the role.

Legal Expert Tip: "Failing to maintain a registered agent can cause your LLC to lose its good standing or be administratively dissolved. In some states, if a lawsuit is served to a non-existent agent, you may lose the case by default judgment within days. Always verify your agent’s status before filing." – Emily Carter, Business Formation Attorney

Step 3: File Articles of Organization with the State

This is the core formation document. The Articles of Organization (called a "Certificate of Formation" in some states) must be filed with the appropriate state agency—typically the Secretary of State. Required information usually includes:

  • LLC name and address of principal place of business
  • Registered agent name and physical address
  • Names and addresses of members (owners) or managers (optional in some states)
  • Purpose of the LLC (often a generic "any lawful business" clause is sufficient)
  • Duration (perpetual or fixed term)

Filing fees range from $40 (e.g., Kentucky) to $520 (Massachusetts). Some states, like California and New York, require additional steps: California mandates publication in a local newspaper for four consecutive weeks and New York requires a publication notice within 120 days of filing. Failure to publish can result in suspension. After filing, you will receive a stamped copy from the state, which officially creates your LLC. Most states process filings in one to three weeks; expedited service is often available for an extra fee ($50–$200).

Step 4: Draft and Adopt an Operating Agreement

Although not required by every state, an operating agreement is arguably the most important legal document for your LLC. This internal contract governs the rights, duties, and responsibilities of members and managers. It covers critical issues like:

  • Capital contributions and ownership percentages
  • Profit and loss allocation
  • Voting rights and decision-making procedures
  • Management structure (member-managed vs. manager-managed)
  • Admission and removal of members
  • Dissolution and buyout provisions

Without an operating agreement, your LLC will be governed by default state laws, which may not reflect your intentions. For instance, in many states, a single-member LLC without an operating agreement may risk being viewed as a "sole proprietorship" by courts, piercing the liability shield. The agreement should be signed by all members. While templates exist online, consult a business attorney if the LLC has multiple members or complex ownership structures.

Step 5: Obtain an EIN and Open a Business Bank Account

The Employer Identification Number (EIN) from the IRS functions like a Social Security number for your LLC. Even a single-member LLC without employees needs an EIN to open a bank account, file certain taxes, and hire contractors. Apply for free at IRS.gov—the process takes about 15 minutes, and the number is issued immediately. With the EIN in hand, open a separate business bank account. Never commingle personal and business funds—this is the most common way courts "pierce the corporate veil" and hold owners personally liable. A dedicated account also simplifies tax preparation. Additionally, consult a CPA about tax elections: by default, a single-member LLC is taxed as a sole proprietorship (Schedule C), while multi-member LLCs are taxed as partnerships. You may also elect S-corp status if beneficial for self-employment tax savings.

Step 6: Obtain Licenses, Permits, and File Annual Reports

An LLC is not a universal business license. Depending on your industry and location, you may need federal, state, and local permits—such as a seller’s permit (for sales tax), a professional license (law, medicine, real estate), or a health department permit (restaurants, salons). Check with your city and county business licensing office. Once your LLC is operating, stay compliant by filing annual or biennial reports (also called "Statement of Information") with the state. Filing fees range from $10 to $800 (California). Late fees can add up quickly. Most states send reminders to your registered agent, but track deadlines yourself as well. Failure to file on time can lead to automatic dissolution. Finally, maintain a formal record of major decisions—minutes of member meetings, changes to the operating agreement, and tax returns—to demonstrate that the LLC is a separate entity, not your alter ego.
