What Is the Lemon Law and How Does It Protect You?

May 26, 2026 7 min read

What Is the Lemon Law?

The lemon law is a set of state and federal regulations designed to protect consumers who purchase or lease a vehicle that fails to meet quality and performance standards. While the specific provisions vary by state, the core principle remains the same: if a vehicle has a substantial defect that the manufacturer is unable to repair after a reasonable number of attempts, the consumer is entitled to a refund, replacement, or cash compensation. The term "lemon" refers to any new vehicle that has persistent issues affecting its use, value, or safety -- think electrical failures, transmission problems, or engine defects that don't go away after multiple trips to the dealership.

The federal Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act adds another layer of protection by allowing consumers to sue manufacturers who fail to honor their written warranties. This law applies to any consumer product, including vehicles, and it can be used in conjunction with state lemon laws to strengthen your case. Many consumers don't realize that lemon law protections also extend to leased vehicles and, in some states, to used cars sold with a warranty. Understanding the basics of these laws is the first step toward holding manufacturers accountable for defective vehicles.

According to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, over 150,000 lemon law cases are filed annually in the United States, with consumers winning the majority of claims that go to arbitration.

What Vehicles Are Covered Under the Lemon Law?

Most state lemon laws cover new cars, trucks, SUVs, and vans purchased or leased for personal use. Some states also include motorcycles, RVs, and even boats, though the rules may differ. Typically, the defect must occur within the first year or within a certain mileage limit -- often 12,000 to 18,000 miles -- but this window varies. If you buy a used vehicle that still has the manufacturer's original warranty, many states will treat it as a new car for lemon law purposes, covering the same defects under the same repair attempt rules.

There are important exclusions. Lemon laws generally do not cover defects caused by owner abuse, neglect, or unauthorized modifications. They also don't cover minor issues that don't affect the vehicle's use, value, or safety -- a squeaky dashboard trim or a loose mirror, for example, won't qualify. However, any defect that substantially impairs the vehicle's operation (like a transmission that slips or a check engine light that stays on) should trigger protection. Before you file a claim, check your state's specific lemon law statute to confirm your vehicle qualifies. Many states also require that you notify the manufacturer in writing before pursuing legal action.

How Many Repair Attempts Are Required Before a Vehicle Is Considered a Lemon?

The number of repair attempts required varies by state, but a common threshold is three to four attempts for the same defect within the warranty period. Some states require only two attempts if the defect is life-threatening -- such as brake failure or a steering system malfunction. Additionally, if the vehicle has been out of service for a cumulative total of 30 days or more (for repairs related to the same defect), that often counts as a qualifying condition. These repair attempts must be made by an authorized dealer or manufacturer-authorized service center.

It's crucial to keep meticulous records of every repair visit. Save all work orders, invoices, and correspondence with the dealer and manufacturer. Note the dates the vehicle was dropped off and picked up, the symptoms you reported, and what was done during each visit. If the dealer claims they fixed the problem but the issue returns, that visit still counts. The law recognizes that a manufacturer should have a reasonable opportunity to fix the defect, but they don't get unlimited chances. If after three or four attempts (or after 30 days out of service) the problem persists, your vehicle likely qualifies as a lemon under your state's definition.

What to Do If You Believe You Have a Lemon

First, do not continue driving the vehicle if it's unsafe. Document every symptom and repair attempt as outlined above. Next, review your vehicle's warranty and your state's lemon law. Most states require you to notify the manufacturer in writing of the defect and allow them one final repair attempt before filing a claim. This is often called a "final cure opportunity" or "last chance letter." Send this letter via certified mail with return receipt requested so you have proof of delivery.

After the final repair attempt fails, you have several options. You can file a claim directly with the manufacturer's consumer arbitration program (many are required by state law to participate in a manufacturer-backed arbitration program like BBB AUTO LINE). Alternatively, you can file a claim with your state's consumer protection agency or hire a lemon law attorney. Attorneys who specialize in lemon law typically work on a contingency fee basis, meaning you pay nothing unless they win your case. They can negotiate a settlement with the manufacturer, which often results in a full refund of the purchase price (minus a deduction for mileage used) or a replacement vehicle of comparable value. Always get any settlement agreement in writing and review it carefully before signing.

Your Legal Rights and Potential Remedies

If your vehicle is deemed a lemon, the manufacturer must provide one of two remedies: a full refund or a replacement vehicle. The refund typically includes the purchase price, sales tax, registration fees, and any finance charges you incurred. A reasonable mileage offset is deducted -- usually between $0.10 and $0.30 per mile driven before the first repair attempt. The manufacturer is also required to pay for towing and repair costs you incurred. In some states, you can choose between a refund and a replacement vehicle; in others, the manufacturer gets to decide. If you choose a replacement, the new vehicle must be of comparable value and come with a fresh warranty.

You also have the right to pursue legal action under the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act if the manufacturer refuses to honor the warranty. If you win, the court can award you attorney's fees and court costs, making it easier to afford legal representation. Many consumers settle directly with the manufacturer through arbitration without ever stepping foot in a courtroom. The process typically takes 30 to 90 days from filing. Remember, lemon laws exist to protect you from being stuck with a defective product. Don't let a dealership or manufacturer brush you off -- the law is on your side.
