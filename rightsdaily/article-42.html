Dog Bite Laws and Owner Liability: What Every Victim Should Know

May 26, 2026 7 min read

1. Strict Liability vs. Negligence: The Two Main Legal Theories

Dog bite laws in the United States generally fall under one of two legal doctrines: strict liability or negligence. In strict liability states, the dog owner is automatically responsible for injuries caused by their dog--regardless of whether the owner knew the dog was dangerous or took precautions. As of 2025, over 30 states, including California, Florida, and New York, impose strict liability for dog bites. The key requirement is that the victim was in a public place or lawfully on private property (i.e., not trespassing).

Negligence-based states, such as Texas and Georgia, require the victim to prove the owner failed to exercise reasonable care in controlling the animal. This often means showing the owner knew or should have known the dog had aggressive tendencies--sometimes called the "one-bite rule." Under this rule, the first bite may give the owner "knowledge" of danger, making them liable for any subsequent attacks. However, many courts now consider the dog's breed, past growling, or aggressive behavior as sufficient notice.

According to the Insurance Information Institute, dog bite claims cost homeowners insurers over $1.1 billion in 2023, with an average payout of $58,545 per claim. Most states hold owners financially responsible for medical bills.

2. What to Do Immediately After a Dog Bite

Your actions in the first hours after a dog bite can significantly affect both your health and your ability to recover compensation. First, prioritize medical care. Even minor punctures can become infected or transmit rabies if the dog isn't vaccinated. Document the wound with photographs from multiple angles--before cleaning and after treatment. Request a copy of the animal's rabies vaccination record from the owner or local animal control.

Second, identify the owner and the dog. Collect names, addresses, phone numbers, and any witness information. If the incident occurred on public property or a neighbor's yard, note the exact location. Report the bite to local animal control or law enforcement. Many jurisdictions require official reports for bites that break the skin, and that report becomes crucial evidence in your legal claim.

Third, preserve evidence. Save the clothing you were wearing--do not wash it--and keep all medical bills, prescription receipts, and records of lost wages. Write a detailed account of the attack while it's fresh in your memory, including the dog's behavior before the bite and any prior incidents you know of. Avoid posting about the incident on social media, as insurers and defense attorneys will monitor those posts.

3. Proving Damages: What You Can Recover

Victims of dog bites can recover both economic and non-economic damages. Economic damages include medical expenses (emergency room visits, surgery, antibiotics, rabies shots, plastic surgery for scarring), lost income while recovering, and future medical costs for ongoing treatment like physical therapy or psychological counseling. Dog attacks frequently cause emotional trauma, especially in children, so therapy costs are often compensable.

Non-economic damages cover pain and suffering, disfigurement, and loss of enjoyment of life. Severe bites that result in permanent scarring--particularly on the face--tend to yield higher settlements. Some states cap non-economic damages in personal injury cases, but dog bite claims are often excluded from caps. Punitive damages may also be available if the owner acted with gross negligence, such as knowingly leaving a dangerous dog off-leash in a public area.

In cases where the dog owner has homeowners or renters insurance, the policy usually covers bite claims up to certain limits. However, some insurers exclude certain breeds (pit bulls, Rottweilers, Dobermans). If the owner is uninsured or underinsured, you may need to pursue a lawsuit against them personally or explore your own underinsured motorist coverage, which sometimes applies to animal attacks.

4. State Variations and the "One-Bite Rule" Exceptions

While many people think the "one-bite rule" means every dog gets one free bite, this is a misconception. Even in traditional negligence states, the rule is often modified by local ordinances. For example, if a city has a leash law and the dog was off-leash at the time of the bite, the owner may be automatically liable under "negligence per se"--meaning the violation of the statute itself establishes negligence. In states like Michigan, the law imposes strict liability for bites when the victim is in a public place, regardless of the dog's prior behavior.

Some states also have "dangerous dog" laws that impose additional penalties if the animal has been declared dangerous by a court or animal control. Owners of dangerous dogs may be required to register the animal, post warning signs, keep it muzzled, or carry extra liability insurance. Failure to comply can result in criminal charges. If a bite occurs where the victim was lawfully on the owner's property--such as a guest or delivery driver--most states extend liability.

Conversely, if the victim was trespassing, teasing, or provoking the dog, liability is often reduced or eliminated. Bites that occur during the commission of a crime (breaking and entering) are generally not compensable. A few states follow the "comparative negligence" model, reducing the victim's recovery by a percentage based on their own fault.

5. How to File a Claim and When to Hire a Lawyer

Most dog bite cases are resolved through insurance negotiations without a lawsuit. The first step is to notify the owner's homeowners or renters insurance company. Provide them with the medical records, photos, and a demand letter outlining your damages. Insurance adjusters will likely make a low initial offer. Do not accept it without understanding the full extent of your injuries--some medical issues (like infections or nerve damage) may not appear for weeks.

You are not required to hire a lawyer, but it is strongly recommended for bites requiring more than a few stitches. An experienced personal injury attorney can handle negotiations, assess the full value of your claim, and file a lawsuit if necessary. Attorney fees are typically contingency-based, meaning you pay only if you win. Look for a lawyer who has specific experience with animal attack cases and has handled claims against the major insurers (State Farm, Allstate, Farmers).

Most states have a statute of limitations for dog bite claims ranging from one to six years. Common time limits: two years in California and Texas, three years in Florida and New York. Missing the deadline permanently bars your claim. If the owner is a government employee (e.g., police dog handler or mail carrier), special rules and shorter deadlines may apply under the Federal Tort Claims Act or state sovereign immunity laws. Always consult a lawyer promptly.
