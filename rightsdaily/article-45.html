• Manufacturing defects -- one particular unit deviates from the intended design (e.g., a power tool with a missing safety guard).
  • Marketing defects -- failure to warn of dangers or inadequate instructions (e.g., a cleaner that doesn't warn against mixing with bleach).

    • If you were injured by a defective product, you may recover medical expenses, lost wages, pain and suffering, and punitive damages in egregious cases. Keep in mind that statutes of limitations for personal injury vary by state, typically one to six years. Don't wait.

    Step-by-Step: What to Do When You Discover a Defect

    Before you escalate to legal action, follow this common-sense process. It preserves your legal rights and often resolves the issue without a lawsuit.

    1. Document everything -- Take photos and videos of the defect, save receipts, keep packaging, and write down timestamps of when you first noticed the problem.
    2. Read the warranty -- Both the express written warranty and any disclaimers. Look for the process to file a warranty claim.
    3. Contact the seller, then the manufacturer -- Start with the store. Under the implied warranty, the retailer is also responsible. Many retailers will take back defective products even if the manufacturer refuses.
    4. Send a formal written complaint -- Email or certified mail. Use the phrase 'breach of implied warranty of merchantability' and 'Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act.' Request a specific resolution: refund, repair, or replacement.
    5. Give them a reasonable time to respond -- Usually 14-30 days. If they ignore you or give a runaround, escalate.
    6. File a complaint with the FTC and your state attorney general -- This creates a paper trail and can trigger regulatory action. The FTC doesn't resolve individual disputes, but a pattern of complaints can lead to enforcement.
    7. Consider small claims court -- For claims under the dollar limit of your state's small claims court (usually $5,000 to $10,000), this is your cheapest option. No lawyer required.

    Small Claims Court: Your Most Practical Option for a Refund

    Most defective product disputes involve under $10,000. That puts them squarely in small claims court territory. Filing fees are typically $30-$100, and you don't need an attorney -- in fact, many small claims courts don't allow lawyers. You present your evidence: photos, receipts, warranty documents, and your own testimony.

    Judges in small claims court are surprisingly consumer-friendly when the defect is clear. They understand that a coffeemaker shouldn't leak after two weeks. Bring any communication with the company showing their refusal to help. If you win, the judge may order the defendant to pay you the purchase price plus court costs, and possibly up to double or triple damages under state consumer protection laws (like California's Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act).

    Pro tip: Before filing, send one last demand letter referencing your intent to sue in small claims. Many companies will settle quickly because the cost of sending a lawyer to small claims court exceeds the cost of just refunding your money.

    When to Call a Product Liability Attorney

    If you were physically injured, if the product caused significant property damage (like a fire that damaged your home), or if the company is a large corporation that won't budge, it's time to call a lawyer. Product liability cases often work on contingency -- you pay nothing upfront and the attorney takes a percentage (typically 30-40%) of the settlement or verdict.

    Look for an attorney who specializes in consumer protection or personal injury with product experience. Many offer free initial consultations. Bring all your documentation. They can help you decide whether to pursue an individual lawsuit or join a class action.

    One more thing: if the product involves safety-related defects, you should also report it to the Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) at SaferProducts.gov. Your report can help prevent others from getting hurt and may trigger a recall.

    Key Takeaways: Know Your Rights, Take Action

    • Implied warranties automatically protect you -- Every purchase comes with a guarantee that the product works as intended. You don't need to sign anything.
    • The Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act lets you sue in federal court for breach of warranty and recover attorney's fees.
    • Strict product liability covers injuries from design, manufacturing, or marketing defects, regardless of negligence.
    • Document everything, start with the seller, and be persistent -- most disputes resolve without court if you know the right legal language.
    • Small claims court is your low-cost weapon for claims under $5,000-$10,000. No lawyer needed.
    • For injuries or high-value claims, hire a product liability attorney -- the consultation is usually free.

    You don't have to just accept a defective product. The law is on your side. Use it.
