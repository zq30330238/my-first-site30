How to Legally Change Your Name: A Step-by-Step Guide

May 28, 2026 9 min read

Step 1: Understand Your State's Requirements for a Legal Name Change

The first step in legally changing your name is to research the specific requirements in your state. Each state has its own procedure, but most follow a similar pattern involving a court petition. Generally, you need to be a resident of the state where you file, and you must have a valid reason for the name change--such as marriage, divorce, personal preference, or safety reasons.

Common reasons that courts accept include: marriage or divorce, adoption, gender identity, religious conversion, or simply wanting a fresh start. However, you cannot change your name to avoid debts, defraud creditors, or evade criminal prosecution. The court will check for any fraudulent intent.

Tip: Visit your local county courthouse website or your state's judicial branch site to find the specific forms needed. Many states provide name change packets with instructions. Filing fees typically range from $150 to $400, depending on your jurisdiction.

According to the American Bar Association, a legal name change typically costs between $150 and $400 in filing fees, not including additional costs for publication or attorney fees.

Step 2: Prepare and File the Petition for Name Change

Once you know your state's requirements, the next step is to complete the name change petition. This document usually includes your current legal name, the requested new name, your date of birth, address, and a brief statement of why you want the change. You will also need to provide proof of identity and residency, such as a driver's license, birth certificate, or passport.

After completing the petition, you must file it with the appropriate court--typically the superior court or family court in the county where you live. File the original with the clerk and pay the filing fee. If you cannot afford the fee, you may apply for a fee waiver by submitting a financial affidavit.

Pro tip: Make at least two copies of the filed petition: one for your records and one to serve on any required parties. Some states require you to serve notice to certain agencies or individuals, such as the Department of Motor Vehicles or a former spouse if you have minor children.

Step 3: Attend the Court Hearing

After your petition is filed, the court will schedule a hearing. In most states, the hearing is relatively brief and formal. You must appear in person unless the judge allows a virtual appearance. At the hearing, the judge will ask you to confirm your identity and the reason for the name change. Be prepared to answer questions about your criminal history, debts, and any objections from interested parties.

If you are changing your name due to marriage or divorce, many states have expedited procedures that may not require a court hearing. However, for other reasons, a hearing is almost always required. Dress appropriately and bring all supporting documents, including the original filed petition and your identification.

The judge will decide based on whether the name change is in the public interest and not intended to defraud. If approved, the judge will sign a court order granting the name change. This document is your legal proof of the new name. Keep the certified copy safe--you will need it to update all your records.

Step 4: Publish Notice of Your Name Change (If Required)

Many states require you to publish a notice of your name change in a local newspaper to inform the public. This requirement helps prevent fraud and gives anyone who might object an opportunity to come forward. The notice typically includes your current name, your new name, and the court case number.

The timeframe for publication varies, but usually you must publish once a week for four consecutive weeks. After the publication period, the newspaper will provide you with an affidavit of publication, which you must file with the court as proof. Some states allow you to request a waiver of publication for safety reasons, such as domestic violence or stalking.

Publishing costs generally range from $50 to $200, depending on your local newspaper's rates. Factor this into your overall name change budget. Once the publication is complete and the court receives the affidavit, the judge's order becomes final and you can begin updating your identification and records.

Step 5: Update Your Identification and Records

With the certified court order in hand, you can now update your name with all relevant institutions. Start with the most critical documents: your Social Security card and driver's license. Visit your local Social Security Administration office with your court order, current identification, and Form SS-5. Your new Social Security card will reflect your new name.

Next, update your driver's license or state ID at your Department of Motor Vehicles. You will need the certified court order, your current license, and sometimes proof of residency. After receiving your new license, proceed to update your passport, bank accounts, credit cards, insurance policies, employer records, voter registration, and utility bills.

Important: Also notify the USPS of your name change if you want mail delivered in your new name. Don't forget to update your professional licenses, medical records, and any legal documents such as your will or power of attorney. Keeping a checklist will help ensure you don't miss any important institution.

Step 6: Notify Other Relevant Parties and Institutions

Beyond government-issued ID, you should inform a wide range of organizations to prevent future complications. This includes your employer (for payroll and tax forms), your children's school, your mortgage lender or landlord, car title and registration, and any subscription services that verify identity.

For financial institutions, you will typically need to provide a copy of the court order and your new ID. Credit bureaus should also be notified so your credit report reflects your new name. You can contact Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion directly.

A common mistake is forgetting to update your name on legal documents such as a will, trust, or power of attorney. If you have a living will or medical directive, those should be revised to avoid confusion during emergencies. Finally, update your email addresses and social media accounts to match your new legal name. Once all updates are complete, you have successfully navigated the legal name change process.
