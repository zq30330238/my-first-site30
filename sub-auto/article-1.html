5 Signs Your Alternator Is Failing and How to Diagnose It

September 21, 2025 5 min read

1. Dimming or Flickering Headlights

Your alternator is the heart of your car's electrical system, converting mechanical energy into electricity to power everything from headlights to the ignition system. When it starts to fail, one of the earliest and most noticeable symptoms is erratic lighting. You may see your headlights dim when you're idling at a stoplight and brighten as you accelerate. This happens because the alternator isn't producing a steady voltage--at low RPMs, the output drops, causing lights to fade. The voltage instability can also affect modern vehicle electronics, causing dashboard warning lights to flicker or the infotainment system to restart unexpectedly.

Interior lights, dashboard gauges, and even the radio display can also flicker or dim unexpectedly. To confirm the issue, you can use a cheap multimeter. With the engine off, a healthy battery reads about 12.6 volts. With the engine running, the alternator should push the voltage to between 13.8 and 14.5 volts. If you see readings below 13 volts while the engine is on--or wildly fluctuating numbers--the alternator is likely on its way out.

According to AAA, electrical system failures account for nearly 10% of all roadside breakdowns in the U.S., and a failing alternator is a leading cause.

Don't ignore flickering lights. If the alternator completely fails, you'll lose all electrical power, including the ignition system, and the car will stall. Address this symptom early to avoid a tow.

2. The Battery Warning Light Comes On

Many drivers see a small red or yellow battery icon on the dashboard and assume the battery itself is dead. In reality, that light is a charge system warning--it illuminates when the voltage from the alternator falls outside the normal range. The light typically turns on when the engine is running and the system detects a voltage below about 13 volts or above 15 volts.

A quick test: if the battery light stays on during driving, but the car starts fine the next morning, the battery is likely healthy and the alternator is the culprit. However, if the light is accompanied by a slow crank on startup, both components may be failing. Most auto parts stores offer free battery and alternator testing. They can check the alternator's output under load (headlights on, AC on) to see if it holds voltage.

Pro tip: If the battery light appears intermittently after driving through deep puddles or after a belt replacement, the alternator may have gotten wet or a new belt could be slipping. But persistent illumination almost always points to a failing alternator diode pack or voltage regulator.

3. Strange Noises from Under the Hood

A healthy alternator runs quietly. When bearings inside the alternator wear out, they produce a distinct whining or grinding noise that changes with engine speed. You'll often hear it as a high-pitched squeal when you first start the car or when accelerating. A grinding or rumbling sound usually indicates bearing failure and imminent seizure.

The noise may also come from a loose or worn serpentine belt that drives the alternator. A belt that's too tight can also strain the alternator bearings. To differentiate, spray a small amount of belt dressing on the belt while the engine is idling. If the noise stops, the belt is the issue. If it continues, the alternator itself is likely at fault. Never ignore bearing noise--a seized alternator can snap the belt, which also powers the water pump and power steering on many vehicles, leading to overheating and loss of steering assist.

If you're handy, you can remove the alternator and spin the pulley by hand. Roughness or side-to-side play confirms worn bearings. Replacement is the only fix.

4. Electrical Accessories Acting Erratically

Your car's alternator supplies juice to everything beyond the basics. When it's weak, you may notice that power windows move slower than usual, the rear defroster takes longer to work, or the cabin heater fan runs sluggishly. The radio or infotainment screen may reset randomly, especially when you use multiple accessories at once (e.g., turning on the headlights while the defroster is running).

These symptoms occur because the alternator cannot supply enough current to meet the demand at low RPMs. In modern cars, the engine control unit (ECU) may even reduce accessory power to conserve voltage for critical systems like fuel injection and ignition. This is often a secondary sign accompanying dimming lights. If you see these erratic behaviors alongside any other symptom above, schedule a diagnosis quickly.

Also check your battery terminals for corrosion. A poor connection can mimic alternator failure by restricting current flow. Clean terminals and tighten connections before replacing any expensive parts.

5. Frequent Jump-Starts and a Dead Battery

One of the most frustrating signs is a battery that requires jump-starting every few days, but then tests as fully charged. If the alternator fails to recharge the battery while driving, the battery will slowly drain and eventually leave you stranded. You jump the car, it runs for a while, but the next morning it's dead again. This cycle clearly indicates the alternator is not recharging.

You can do a simple system test at home. With the engine running, turn on the headlights, the heater fan on high, and the rear defroster. Then measure voltage at the battery with a multimeter. If it stays above 13.5V, the alternator is likely okay. If it drops below 12.5V under load, the alternator is failing. Also check the alternator's amperage output if you have a clamp meter--most modern alternators should produce 80-120 amps depending on the vehicle.

Don't ignore the warning. Continuing to drive with a bad alternator can also damage your battery. Once the alternator fails completely, you'll be stranded. Replacing an alternator is usually a straightforward job for a mechanic, costing between $300 and $800 including parts and labor. Preventative replacement at the first sign saves you the headache of an unexpected breakdown.
