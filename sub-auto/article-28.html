BYD Seal Review: The Electric Sedan Taking on Tesla Model 3 Worldwide

July 3, 2022 8 min read

1. Design and Exterior: Aero‑Sculpted Sophistication

The BYD Seal adopts a low‑slung, coupe‑like profile that immediately signals its performance intentions. With a drag coefficient of just 0.219 Cd, it edges out the Tesla Model 3 (0.23 Cd) in wind‑tunnel efficiency. The front fascia features a sleek LED light bar and sculpted air intakes, while the flush door handles and frameless windows enhance the aerodynamic story.

At 4,800 mm long with a 2,920 mm wheelbase, the Seal is slightly larger than the Model 3, translating to a more spacious rear seat and a 402‑liter trunk (plus a 53‑liter frunk). The fastback silhouette doesn't just look good--it reduces wind noise at highway speeds and contributes to the car's impressive 650 km CLTC range on the long‑range model.

Build quality impresses for its price point: panel gaps are consistent, paint depth meets premium standards, and the doors close with a solid thunk that belies the car's ~$35k starting price. Outside of China, BYD has equipped the Seal with heat‑pump thermal management as standard, ensuring consistent range in cold climates--a common Achilles' heel for many EVs.

Key stat: The BYD Seal's 0.219 Cd drag coefficient beats the Tesla Model 3's 0.23 Cd, marking it as one of the most aerodynamically efficient production sedans under $40,000.

2. Powertrain and Performance: Instant Torque, Real‑World Thrills

The Seal is available in rear‑wheel‑drive or all‑wheel‑drive configurations. The base RWD model produces 308 hp and 360 Nm of torque, hitting 60 mph in 5.9 seconds. Opt for the AWD Performance variant, and you get 523 hp and 670 Nm, launching to 60 mph in just 3.8 seconds. That's quicker than the Tesla Model 3 Long Range AWD (4.2 seconds) and nearly ties the Model 3 Performance.

What sets the Seal apart is its 800‑volt electrical architecture. Not only does it enable ultra‑fast DC charging at up to 150 kW (10%-80% in 37 minutes), but it also improves overall efficiency by reducing energy losses. The 82.5 kWh LFP Blade Battery (BYD's own chemistry) offers exceptional thermal stability and cell‑to‑body integration, which increases structural rigidity while lowering the center of gravity.

Real‑world driving reveals a well‑tuned suspension: MacPherson struts up front and a five‑link rear setup absorb bumps without sacrificing cornering composure. The steering is light but accurate, and regenerative braking offers four selectable levels, including a one‑pedal mode that brings the car to a complete stop.

During our week-long test of the Seal AWD Performance variant, we found the acceleration to be relentless yet linear—there is no sudden torque spike, which makes the car easy to modulate in city traffic. On winding mountain roads, the low center of gravity (thanks to the Blade Battery integrated into the floor) keeps body roll remarkably flat, inspiring confidence through fast sweepers. The brake-by-wire system blends regenerative and friction braking seamlessly, though the pedal feel is slightly artificial compared to a traditional hydraulic setup. In Sport mode, the simulated engine sound piped through the speakers adds a subtle futuristic whine that enhances the driving experience without feeling gimmicky. Owners coming from a Tesla Model 3 will notice that the Seal’s suspension is tuned more for comfort than razor-edge dynamics, but the trade-off is a noticeably quieter and more compliant ride on rough pavement.

3. Interior and Technology: Minimalist Yet Feature‑Rich

Step inside, and you're greeted by a 15.6‑inch rotating infotainment display--just like in the BYD Atto 3--running the Dalink OS. The screen can pivot between portrait and landscape orientation, a unique party trick that Tesla can't match. Wireless Apple CarPlay and Android Auto are standard, and the UI is responsive with crisp 1080p graphics.

The cabin materials are a mix of soft‑touch plastics, faux leather, and metallic accents. Heated and ventilated front seats with memory settings come standard on all trims. A panoramic glass roof with UV‑protective coating floods the interior with light while blocking 99% of infrared radiation.

Driver assistance features include adaptive cruise control, lane‑keeping assist, blind‑spot monitoring, and automatic emergency braking. While not as polished as Tesla's Autopilot for highway lane changes, the system is competent and the lane‑centering is smoother than most competitors in the $35k-$45k segment.

4. Range, Charging, and Practicality: Everyday Usability

The CLTC range figures are 550 km (RWD) and 650 km (AWD); real‑world EPA‑estimated range is around 390 miles for the RWD and 340 miles for the AWD. In mixed driving, we observed 4.1 miles per kWh, which is excellent for a sedan this size. The heat pump helps maintain efficiency in winter; during a 20°F test, range dropped only 12% compared to the EPA rating.

Charging infrastructure remains a challenge outside of China: BYD does not have its own Supercharger network. However, the Seal supports CCS and can use third‑party DC fast chargers. In Europe and Southeast Asia, partnerships with local charging networks are expanding, but in the US, CCS stations (especially 350 kW units) are still less ubiquitous than Tesla's Superchargers.

Home charging via a 7.2 kW onboard charger takes about 11.5 hours to fully recharge the 82.5 kWh battery from empty. For daily commuting, a standard Level 2 charger easily replenishes the battery overnight.

One of the Seal's hidden strengths is its charging curve. While peak power tops out at 150 kW, the car sustains above 120 kW from 10% to nearly 55% state of charge, meaning a 10-to-80% session takes just 37 minutes in optimal conditions. By comparison, the Tesla Model 3 Long Range charges at up to 250 kW but its curve drops below 100 kW after 30%, giving the Seal a real-world charging speed that is closer than the peak numbers suggest. In a head-to-head test on a 350 kW CCS charger, the Seal added 180 miles of range in 25 minutes versus 195 miles for the Model 3—a difference of only 8% despite the Tesla's higher peak. Efficiency-wise, the Seal's 4.1 mi/kWh in mixed driving edges ahead of the Model 3's 3.9 mi/kWh, thanks to the 800-volt architecture reducing electrical losses. Over 15,000 miles annually, that translates to roughly $80 in electricity savings at the US average rate of $0.14/kWh.

5. Verdict: Who Should Buy the BYD Seal?

The BYD Seal offers an impressive mix of design, range, performance, and value. Starting at around $35,000 (before incentives), it undercuts the Tesla Model 3 by several thousand dollars while delivering comparable or superior spec sheet numbers. The 800‑volt architecture and Blade Battery give it a safety and charging edge over many rivals.

Where it falls short is software polish, over‑the‑air update frequency, and charging network maturity. If you live in a region with robust CCS infrastructure (Europe, parts of Asia) and value a quiet, comfortable cabin with a flashy infotainment screen, the Seal is a seriously compelling choice. If you rely on Tesla's Supercharger network and want the most mature autonomous driving features, the Model 3 still holds the crown--but the gap is narrowing fast.
