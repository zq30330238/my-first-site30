5 Tire Maintenance Tips to Extend Life and Improve Safety

January 2, 2023 5 min read

Your car's tires are the only contact between your vehicle and the road. Proper tire maintenance not only extends the life of your tires but also directly impacts your safety, fuel economy, and ride comfort. According to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA), tire-related crashes cause nearly 200 fatalities annually in the United States. Yet many drivers overlook basic tire care. In this guide, we cover five critical tire maintenance tasks every driver should perform regularly.

Whether you drive a sedan, SUV, or truck, these tips will help you get the most out of your tires while keeping you and your passengers safe. Let's dive in.

1. Maintain Proper Tire Pressure

One of the simplest yet most neglected aspects of tire maintenance is checking tire pressure. Under-inflated tires create excessive rolling resistance, which reduces fuel efficiency and causes premature tread wear on the outer edges. Over-inflated tires lead to a harsh ride and center-tread wear, and they're more prone to damage from potholes or debris.

The recommended pressure for your vehicle is listed on a sticker inside the driver's door jamb or in the owner's manual--not on the tire sidewall. Use a reliable tire gauge to check pressure when the tires are cold (before driving or at least three hours after stopping). A drop of just 5 psi below the recommended level can reduce fuel economy by up to 2% and shorten tire life by 15%.

Expert Tip: The NHTSA reports that about one in four passenger vehicles are driven with at least one significantly under-inflated tire. Check your tire pressure at least once a month, and always before long trips.

Invest in a digital tire gauge for accuracy, and keep a portable air compressor in your trunk. Many gas stations also offer free air, but their gauges are often inaccurate. If you drive in changing seasons, remember that tire pressure drops approximately 1 psi for every 10°F decrease in temperature.

2. Rotate Your Tires on Schedule

Tire rotation involves moving each tire to a different position on the vehicle (e.g., front-left to rear-right) to ensure even wear. Front tires on front-wheel-drive cars wear faster because they handle steering, acceleration, and braking. Similarly, rear tires on rear-wheel-drive cars experience more stress.

Most manufacturers recommend rotating tires every 5,000 to 7,500 miles, or every other oil change. For all-wheel-drive vehicles, more frequent rotations may be necessary--check your owner's manual. Not rotating tires can lead to uneven wear patterns that reduce usable tread life and compromise handling.

Professional tire rotation costs between $20 and $50, but many tire shops include it free when you purchase tires from them. If you're handy, you can rotate tires yourself using a jack, jack stands, and a torque wrench--just ensure you follow the correct pattern for your vehicle's drivetrain.

3. Check and Adjust Wheel Alignment

Wheel alignment refers to the angles of your tires relative to the road and each other. Proper alignment keeps your car tracking straight and prevents uneven tire wear. Symptoms of misalignment include your car pulling to one side, a crooked steering wheel when driving straight, or rapid wear on the inside or outside edges of the tread.

Alignment is affected by hitting curbs, potholes, or rough road surfaces. Over time, even normal driving can knock alignment out of spec. Most manufacturers suggest checking alignment annually or whenever you notice symptoms. A professional alignment typically costs $75-$150 and can extend tire life by thousands of miles.

One often-overlooked factor: after replacing tires, always have the alignment checked. New tires exacerbate any misalignment because their deeper tread amplifies uneven pressure. A well-aligned car also improves fuel efficiency by reducing rolling resistance.

4. Inspect Tread Depth and Sidewalls

Your tires' tread provides the grip needed for braking, cornering, and driving in rain or snow. In the United States, the legal minimum tread depth is 2/32 of an inch (1.6 mm). Below that, stopping distances increase dramatically and hydroplaning risk soars.

Use the penny test: insert a penny into the tread with Lincoln's head facing down. If you can see the top of his head, the tread is too shallow. For better accuracy, use a tread depth gauge--available for under $10. More importantly, inspect the sidewalls for cuts, bulges, or cracks. Sidewall damage can cause sudden blowouts, especially at highway speeds.

Many experts recommend replacing tires when tread reaches 4/32 of an inch (3.2 mm) in wet climates. A set of four new tires costs $400-$1,200 depending on size and brand, which is far less expensive than an accident or roadside emergency. Always check your tires visually before any long trip.

5. Balance Tires After Installation or Vibrations

Tire balancing corrects weight imbalances between the tire and wheel assembly. Unbalanced tires cause vibrations that can be felt in the steering wheel, floorboard, or seat. These vibrations not only annoy drivers but also accelerate wear on suspension components like ball joints and tie rods.

Balancing is performed when new tires are mounted, but it may also become necessary if a wheel weight falls off or after hitting a severe impact. A typical balancing service costs $15-$40 per tire and should be done every time you get new tires or rotate them. If you notice persistent vibration at certain speeds, have the balance checked immediately.

Driving on unbalanced tires for an extended period can lead to a bumpy wear pattern known as cupping, which ruins the tire and forces premature replacement. Tire shop technicians use a spin-balancing machine to measure uneven spots and attach small weights to the rim. After balancing, your car will ride smoother and your tires will last longer.

By following these five maintenance steps--checking pressure, rotating, aligning, inspecting, and balancing--you can add up to 15,000 miles to your tire set and significantly reduce the risk of tire-related failures. Make tire care a regular part of your vehicle routine.
