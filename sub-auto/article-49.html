XPeng G6 Review: The Model Y Competitor with 800V Charging That Actually Works

May 24, 2026 8 min read

1. 800V SEPA 2.0 Platform: Real-World Charging Speed That Delivers

The XPeng G6 is built on the company's second-generation SEPA 2.0 (Smart Electric Platform Architecture), which features a native 800V electrical architecture. This isn't just a marketing badge -- it translates directly to measurable charging performance. During independent testing at a 350 kW DC fast charger, the G6 charged from 10% to 80% state of charge in just 18 minutes and 42 seconds. That's within striking distance of the Hyundai Ioniq 5's 18-minute claim and significantly faster than the Tesla Model Y Long Range, which typically takes 25-30 minutes for the same charge window.

The secret lies in the G6's silicon carbide (SiC) power modules and a battery pack that can accept up to 480 kW peak charging power. While most current chargers cap at 350 kW, the G6 still pulls a sustained 280 kW from 10% to 40%, tapering to 120 kW by 80%. This means a 10-minute stop adds approximately 250 miles of range -- enough for most daily commutes and regional trips. The 800V system also reduces energy loss during charging by roughly 30% compared to 400V architectures, lowering heat generation and preserving battery health over time.

Key Stat: The XPeng G6 charges from 10% to 80% in under 19 minutes at a 350 kW charger -- faster than a Tesla Model Y and on par with the Hyundai Ioniq 5, making it one of the quickest-charging SUVs under $50,000.

For home charging, the G6 supports up to 11 kW AC charging, replenishing a full 87.5 kWh battery in about 8 hours. The battery pack uses a lithium iron phosphate (LFP) chemistry in the standard range model and a nickel manganese cobalt (NMC) pack in the long-range version. LFP offers better longevity and thermal stability, while NMC provides higher energy density for extended range. The G6 Long Range achieves an EPA-estimated 320 miles on a full charge, putting it squarely in Model Y Long Range territory.

2. XNGP Autonomous Driving vs Tesla FSD: A Capability Comparison

XPeng's XNGP (XPeng Navigation Guided Pilot) system is the company's answer to Tesla's Full Self-Driving (FSD) suite. In urban environments, XNGP currently supports navigation-based driving on city streets in over 50 Chinese cities, with plans to expand to 200+ by end of 2026. The system handles traffic lights, unprotected left turns, pedestrian crossings, and lane changes with a confidence that rivals Tesla FSD Beta. During a 30-minute urban test route in Shanghai, the G6 completed 12 unprotected turns without driver intervention, compared to 9 for a Tesla Model Y on FSD 12.3 in similar conditions.

On highways, XNGP is exceptionally polished. It maintains lane centering with minimal ping-ponging, executes smooth lane changes when the driver activates the turn signal, and adjusts speed for curves and traffic flow. The system uses a combination of 12 ultrasonic sensors, 5 millimeter-wave radars, and 11 cameras -- including two lidar units mounted in the front bumper. Lidar gives XNGP a distinct advantage in low-light conditions and heavy rain, where Tesla's vision-only approach sometimes struggles. In a controlled test at dusk, the G6 detected a stationary obstacle at 150 meters, while the Model Y's FSD detected it at 110 meters.

However, Tesla FSD still leads in overall maturity and geographic coverage. FSD is available in North America and parts of Europe, while XNGP is currently limited to China, with a phased rollout planned for Europe starting in late 2026. XPeng has confirmed that the G6's hardware is capable of Level 4 autonomy with over-the-air updates, but regulatory approvals will dictate the timeline. For now, XNGP is a compelling system that matches or exceeds FSD in specific scenarios, especially urban navigation with lidar assistance.

3. Build Quality, Interior Space, and Ride Comfort Compared to Model Y and Ioniq 5

Step inside the XPeng G6, and the first impression is one of surprising maturity. The cabin uses soft-touch materials on the dashboard, door panels, and center console, with real metal accents and a clean, minimalist design. Panel gaps are tight and consistent -- a notable improvement over early XPeng models. The front seats are heavily bolstered yet comfortable for long drives, with 12-way power adjustment, heating, ventilation, and massage functions on higher trims. Rear seat space is generous: 38.5 inches of legroom beats the Model Y's 38.0 inches and matches the Ioniq 5's 38.7 inches. Headroom is ample even for six-foot passengers, thanks to the flat roofline.

Ride comfort is where the G6 truly shines. The suspension uses a front double-wishbone and rear multi-link setup with frequency-selective damping. On rough pavement, the G6 absorbs impacts with a plushness that the Model Y lacks -- the Tesla tends to transmit sharp bumps into the cabin. The Ioniq 5 is slightly more compliant, but the G6 strikes a better balance between comfort and body control. Steering is light but accurate, with three selectable modes. The G6's 4,700-pound curb weight is noticeable in tight corners, but the low center of gravity from the underfloor battery pack keeps body roll in check.

Cargo space is competitive: 25.6 cubic feet behind the rear seats and 60.2 cubic feet with the seats folded. That's slightly less than the Model Y's 30.2/76.2 cubic feet but more than the Ioniq 5's 27.2/59.3 cubic feet. The G6 also features a front trunk (frunk) with 4.2 cubic feet of storage, perfect for charging cables or a small suitcase. Overall, the G6's interior quality and space are on par with or better than the Model Y, and it offers a more comfortable ride than either the Tesla or the Hyundai.

4. European Expansion Timeline, Pricing, and Warranty Coverage

XPeng officially entered the European market in 2024 with the G9 and P7 models, but the G6 is the volume play. Deliveries began in Norway, Sweden, Denmark, and the Netherlands in early 2026, with Germany, France, and the UK following by mid-2026. The G6 is priced from €44,990 in Germany for the Standard Range model (LFP battery, 280 miles WLTP) and €49,990 for the Long Range (NMC battery, 320 miles WLTP). That undercuts the Tesla Model Y Long Range (€52,990 in Germany) by roughly €3,000 and the Hyundai Ioniq 5 (€48,900) by about €4,000. In the UK, pricing starts at £39,990, directly competing with the Model Y at £44,990.

Warranty coverage is a strong selling point: XPeng offers a 5-year/100,000-mile vehicle warranty, an 8-year/150,000-mile battery warranty, and a 12-year anti-corrosion warranty. The battery warranty guarantees at least 70% capacity retention over the period. XPeng also includes 3 years of free scheduled maintenance and 2 years of free public charging at partner networks in select European markets. The company has established a network of 120 service centers across Europe as of May 2026, with plans to double that by 2028.

Charging infrastructure partnerships include Ionity, Fastned, and TotalEnergies, giving G6 owners access to over 10,000 fast-charging points across Europe. The G6's 800V architecture is fully compatible with CCS2 chargers, and XPeng's navigation system automatically routes to compatible chargers and preconditions the battery for optimal charging speed. For buyers concerned about resale value, XPeng offers a guaranteed buyback program in select markets, promising 60% of the purchase price after 3 years -- a move designed to build consumer confidence in a relatively new brand.
