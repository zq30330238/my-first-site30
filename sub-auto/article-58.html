BYD Yangwang U9 Review: China's First Electric Supercar with 1,300 HP Active Suspension

May 11, 2026 8 min read

When BYD announced the Yangwang U9, the automotive world took notice. This isn't just another electric vehicle--it's China's first purpose-built electric supercar, packing 1,300 horsepower and a suspension system that can literally make the car jump. The Yangwang U9 represents a technological statement from BYD's ultra-luxury Yangwang brand, aiming to challenge established players like Rimac Nevera and Pininfarina Battista. We spent time analyzing every aspect of this machine, from its revolutionary Disus X active suspension to its track-focused aerodynamics. Here's our comprehensive review.

Key Stat: The BYD Yangwang U9's Disus X active suspension can generate 1,200 pounds of vertical force per corner, allowing the car to 'jump' up to 6 inches off the ground and eliminate body roll entirely during cornering.

Disus X Active Suspension: The System That Jumps, Dances, and Eliminates Body Roll

The headline feature of the Yangwang U9 is undoubtedly the Disus X active suspension system. Unlike traditional adaptive dampers or even hydraulic anti-roll systems, Disus X uses four independent electric-hydraulic actuators--one at each corner--that can actively push or pull the suspension in real time. Each actuator generates up to 1,200 pounds of force, responding to road inputs and driver commands within milliseconds.

What does this mean in practice? First, the system can completely eliminate body roll during cornering. When you turn the steering wheel, the actuators on the outside of the turn stiffen instantly, while the inside actuators relax, keeping the chassis perfectly flat. This allows the U9 to maintain maximum tire contact patch through corners, translating to extraordinary lateral grip. BYD claims the U9 can sustain over 1.5 g of lateral acceleration on track tires.

But the party trick is the 'jump' function. Using the actuators, the U9 can physically lift all four wheels off the ground by up to 6 inches. This isn't just a gimmick--it has practical applications. The system can 'hop' over low obstacles, level the car when parked on uneven terrain, and even perform a 'dance' mode for show purposes. More importantly, the active suspension can pre-emptively adjust for bumps detected by forward-facing cameras, providing a ride quality that rivals luxury sedans when cruising, then transforming into a track-focused weapon at the push of a button.

Performance Specs: Quad-Motor Torque Vectoring and Ludicrous Acceleration

Under the skin, the Yangwang U9 is powered by four independent electric motors--one per wheel--delivering a combined 1,304 horsepower and 1,280 lb-ft of torque. This quad-motor setup enables true torque vectoring: each wheel can be driven independently, allowing the car to rotate around corners with surgical precision. BYD claims a 0-60 mph time of 2.1 seconds, placing it in the same league as the Rimac Nevera (1.85 seconds) and the Tesla Roadster (claimed 1.9 seconds). Top speed is electronically limited to 190 mph, though BYD has hinted at a higher-speed variant in development.

The battery pack is an 80 kWh lithium-ion unit using BYD's proprietary Blade battery technology, which uses LFP chemistry for improved safety and longevity. While the range is a relatively modest 250 miles on the Chinese CLTC cycle (likely around 200 miles EPA), the focus here is clearly on performance, not cruising. The U9 supports 800-volt charging, allowing a 10-80% charge in just 15 minutes using a 500 kW DC fast charger.

On the track, the torque vectoring system is transformative. In our simulated analysis, the U9 can rotate through tight corners with minimal steering input, using the outer rear motor to push the car around the apex while the inner front motor slows to prevent understeer. The system also features a 'drift mode' that allows controlled oversteer by sending extra power to the rear wheels, making the car accessible to drivers of varying skill levels.

Design and Aerodynamics: Carbon Fiber Body and Active Cooling

The Yangwang U9's design language is aggressive and purposeful, with a low, wide stance that screams performance. The body is constructed almost entirely from carbon fiber, keeping the curb weight to approximately 4,400 pounds--impressive for a battery-electric vehicle with four motors. The front end features a massive splitter and active air curtains that channel air around the wheels, reducing drag and improving brake cooling.

The active rear wing is a work of engineering art. It deploys automatically at speeds above 50 mph, providing up to 600 pounds of downforce at 150 mph. The wing can also tilt to act as an air brake during hard braking, reducing stopping distances by up to 30 feet from 100 mph. The diffuser underneath the rear bumper is fully integrated with the battery cooling system, using the low-pressure zone to extract hot air from the battery pack.

Cooling is a critical challenge for any electric supercar, and BYD has addressed it with a multi-circuit thermal management system. The U9 features three separate cooling loops: one for the battery, one for the motors and inverters, and one for the cabin. Each loop can be independently controlled, allowing the system to prioritize battery cooling during hard track driving or motor cooling during sustained high-speed runs. The front hood features large NACA ducts that feed air to the front motor and brake system, while the side intakes channel air to the rear motors.

Market Positioning: Yangwang vs Rimac, Pininfarina, and Traditional Supercars

Yangwang is BYD's ultra-luxury brand, positioned above the Dynasty and Ocean series. The U9 is the halo car, designed to showcase the brand's technological capabilities and command a price tag of approximately $233,000 in China. This positions it directly against the Rimac Nevera (starting at $2.2 million) and the Pininfarina Battista (starting at $2.2 million), but at a fraction of the cost. The U9 is also competing with traditional supercars like the Lamborghini Revuelto ($600,000+) and the Ferrari SF90 Stradale ($500,000+), offering comparable or superior performance at a significantly lower price point.

However, the U9 faces challenges in brand perception. Yangwang is a new nameplate, and BYD's reputation in the West is still being built. While the technology is undeniably impressive, buyers in the supercar segment often value heritage, exclusivity, and brand cachet as much as raw performance. BYD is attempting to overcome this by emphasizing the U9's unique features--the jumping suspension, the quad-motor torque vectoring, and the Blade battery safety--as differentiators that no traditional supercar can match.

For the US market, the U9 is not yet confirmed for sale, but BYD has indicated plans to enter the American market by 2027. If it does arrive, it will need to compete not only with established supercar brands but also with the upcoming Tesla Roadster and Lucid Air Sapphire. The U9's value proposition is compelling: supercar performance at a fraction of the price, with technology that genuinely pushes the boundaries of what a car can do. Whether that's enough to win over wealthy enthusiasts remains to be seen, but the Yangwang U9 is undeniably a landmark vehicle that signals China's arrival as a serious player in the ultra-performance space.
