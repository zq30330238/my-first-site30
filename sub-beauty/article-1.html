How to Build a Skincare Routine for Your Skin Type: The Complete Guide

7 min read May 28, 2026 Jordan Myers

Key Takeaways

  • Identify your skin type before choosing products — the wrong routine can worsen existing concerns like breakouts or dryness
  • A complete AM routine follows five steps: cleanse, tone, serum, moisturize, and SPF in that exact order
  • A complete PM routine uses double cleansing, tone, targeted treatment, and a richer moisturizer for overnight repair
  • Choose ingredients based on your skin type: niacinamide for oily, ceramides for dry, centella for sensitive skin
  • Consistency matters more than the number of products — a simple routine done daily outperforms a complex one done sporadically

Skincare is not one-size-fits-all. The products your best friend swears by might break you out, and the moisturizer a dermatologist recommends for dry skin could leave your combination skin feeling greasy. The difference comes down to one factor: your skin type.

Building an effective skincare routine starts with knowing whether your skin is normal, dry, oily, combination, or sensitive. Once you identify your type, selecting the right products becomes straightforward. This guide walks through every step of the process — from the simple at-home test that reveals your skin type to the specific ingredients that address each type's unique needs.

Identifying Your Skin Type

Your skin type is determined largely by genetics, though it can change with age, climate, hormones, and lifestyle. The five primary skin types each have distinct characteristics that influence how your skin reacts to cleansers, moisturizers, and active ingredients.

Normal skin is balanced — not too oily and not too dry. It has a smooth texture, small pores, and no frequent breakouts or sensitivity. Dry skin produces less sebum than normal skin, leading to a tight feeling after washing, visible flaking, rough patches, and fine lines. Oily skin produces excess sebum, resulting in a shiny appearance, enlarged pores, blackheads, and a higher tendency toward acne. Combination skin is the most common type — the T-zone (forehead, nose, and chin) is oily while the cheeks are normal or dry. Sensitive skin reacts easily to products and environmental factors with redness, stinging, burning, or itching.

The Bare-Faced Test

The simplest way to determine your skin type at home takes about 30 minutes. Wash your face with a gentle cleanser and pat it dry. Do not apply any products. After 30 minutes, examine your skin. If your entire face feels tight and looks dull, you likely have dry skin. If your T-zone is shiny but your cheeks feel comfortable, you have combination skin. If your entire face looks shiny, your skin is oily. If your skin feels comfortable with no shine or tightness, you have normal skin. If you experience redness or stinging during the waiting period, your skin is sensitive. Building Your AM Skincare Routine

The goal of a morning routine is protection. Your skin needs defense against UV rays, pollution, and environmental stressors throughout the day. An effective AM routine follows five steps applied in a specific order from thinnest to thickest consistency.

Step 1: Cleanse

Morning cleansing removes sweat, oil, and residue from overnight products. For normal and dry skin, a splash of water or a cream cleanser is enough. Oily and combination skin benefit from a gentle foaming or gel cleanser. Sensitive skin should use a milky or non-foaming cleanser with soothing ingredients. Avoid stripping cleansers that leave your skin feeling tight.

Step 2: Tone

A hydrating toner restores your skin's pH balance after cleansing and preps the surface to absorb serums more effectively. Look for alcohol-free formulas with ingredients like glycerin, niacinamide, or rose water. Apply with clean hands or a cotton pad, pressing gently into the skin rather than rubbing.

Step 3: Serum

Morning serums should focus on antioxidants. Vitamin C is the gold standard — it neutralizes free radicals from UV exposure and pollution, brightens the complexion, and supports collagen production. Other beneficial morning ingredients include hyaluronic acid for hydration, niacinamide for oil control, and peptides for firmness.

"Vitamin C serum in the morning is the single most impactful addition most people can make to their routine. It works synergistically with sunscreen to provide more comprehensive UV protection than sunscreen alone. The combination can quadruple your skin's defense against photoaging."

Step 4: Moisturize

Even oily skin needs moisturizer. When the skin is dehydrated, it compensates by producing more oil, creating a vicious cycle. Choose a moisturizer based on your skin type: lightweight gel or water-based formulas for oily and combination skin, richer cream-based formulas for dry skin, and fragrance-free options for sensitive skin.

Step 5: SPF

Sunscreen is the most critical step in any morning routine. UV exposure causes 80 percent of visible skin aging and is the primary preventable cause of skin cancer. Use a broad-spectrum SPF 30 or higher every single day, even when it is cloudy or you are staying indoors. Chemical sunscreens absorb into the skin and feel lightweight, while physical sunscreens (zinc oxide or titanium dioxide) sit on top and are better suited for sensitive skin.

Building Your PM Skincare Routine

The evening routine focuses on repair. While you sleep, your skin enters a recovery mode where cell turnover increases and active ingredients work most effectively. A PM routine has four main steps.

Step 1: Double Cleanse

Double cleansing is the most effective way to remove sunscreen, makeup, and daily grime. Start with an oil-based cleanser or cleansing balm to dissolve oil-based impurities. Follow with a water-based cleanser to remove sweat and any residue from the first step. This two-step method ensures your skin is completely clean without stripping its natural barrier. Those with very dry or sensitive skin can skip the oil cleanse on mornings but should use it every evening.

Step 2: Tone

Same as the morning — apply a hydrating, alcohol-free toner to balance pH and prep for treatment products. Exfoliating toners containing AHAs or BHAs can be used in the evening 2 to 3 times per week instead of your regular hydrating toner.

Step 3: Treatment

Evening treatments are where targeted results happen. Retinol (vitamin A) is the most researched anti-aging ingredient and also effective for acne. Start with a low concentration once per week and build up. Other evening treatment options include glycolic acid for texture, salicylic acid for clogged pores, and azelaic acid for redness. Use only one active treatment per evening to avoid irritation.

Step 4: Moisturize

Your night moisturizer should be richer and more nourishing than your day moisturizer. Look for ingredients like ceramides, squalane, shea butter, and peptides that support the skin barrier overnight. Apply a slightly thicker layer than you would in the morning and allow it to absorb before sleeping.

Ingredient Recommendations by Skin Type

Choosing the right ingredients is the difference between a routine that transforms your skin and one that simply sits on the surface. Each skin type benefits from specific active ingredients while potentially reacting poorly to others.

For Dry Skin

Dry skin needs ingredients that reinforce the skin barrier and trap moisture. Look for hyaluronic acid, which holds up to 1,000 times its weight in water and delivers deep hydration when applied to damp skin. Ceramides are lipid molecules that fill the gaps between skin cells, preventing moisture loss. Squalane mimics the skin's natural sebum and provides lightweight, non-greasy hydration. Avoid alcohol-based toners, foaming cleansers with sulfates, and high-concentration AHAs that can strip the skin further.

For Oily Skin

Oily skin responds well to ingredients that regulate sebum production and keep pores clear. Niacinamide (vitamin B3) reduces oil production, improves pore appearance, and strengthens the skin barrier. Salicylic acid (BHA) penetrates deep into pores to dissolve sebum and prevent blackheads. Clay-based masks once or twice per week absorb excess oil. Avoid heavy creams with coconut oil, shea butter, or mineral oil that can clog pores and trigger breakouts.

"The biggest mistake people with oily skin make is skipping moisturizer. When you strip oil with harsh cleansers and skip hydration, your skin overcompensates by producing even more oil. A lightweight, oil-free moisturizer with niacinamide is essential for breaking this cycle."

For Combination Skin

Combination skin requires a balanced approach. Gel-based moisturizers work well because they hydrate without adding weight to the oily T-zone or insufficient moisture to the dry cheeks. Hyaluronic acid and glycerin are excellent hydrators that benefit both zones. Use a gentle foaming cleanser and consider multi-moisturizing — a lightweight gel on the T-zone and a richer cream on the cheeks. For a complete product list, see our guide to moisturizer ingredients and product types.

For Sensitive Skin

Sensitive skin needs ingredients that calm and protect. Centella asiatica (cica) is a powerful anti-inflammatory that reduces redness and supports healing. Panthenol (vitamin B5) soothes irritation and strengthens the skin barrier. Oat extract provides gentle, non-irritating moisture. Allantoin promotes cell regeneration without stinging. Avoid essential oils, fragrances, high-concentration acids, and any product with more than 10 ingredients. Patch test every new product on your inner arm for one week before applying it to your face.

For Normal Skin

Normal skin is the most resilient type but still benefits from targeted maintenance. Focus on antioxidants like vitamin C and E for environmental protection, peptides to support collagen, and consistent hydration. Normal skin can tolerate a wider range of active ingredients, making it a good candidate for preventive anti-aging routines that include retinol and glycolic acid at low concentrations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common concerns about building a skincare routine tailored to your skin type

How long should I wait between applying each skincare product?

Wait about 30 to 60 seconds between each step to allow the product to absorb. For leave-on exfoliants or prescription retinoids, wait 15 to 20 minutes before applying the next layer. This prevents pilling and ensures each product works at full strength without being diluted by the layer above it.

Do I need to use different moisturizers for day and night?

Using a separate day and night moisturizer is recommended but not mandatory. A daytime moisturizer should be lightweight and ideally contain SPF or sit well under sunscreen. A night moisturizer can be richer and contain reparative ingredients like peptides, ceramides, or retinol since the skin repairs itself during sleep.

How often should I exfoliate my skin?

For most skin types, exfoliating 2 to 3 times per week is sufficient. Those with oily skin may tolerate up to 4 times weekly, while sensitive or dry skin should stick to once per week. Over-exfoliating damages the skin barrier and leads to redness, irritation, and breakouts. Always follow exfoliation with a soothing moisturizer.

Is toner a necessary step in a skincare routine?

Toner is not strictly necessary, but it offers benefits that many find worthwhile. Modern hydrating toners restore pH balance after cleansing and prep the skin to absorb serums more effectively. Those with oily or acne-prone skin benefit most from toner, while very dry or sensitive skin can skip it or use a gentle mist instead.

At what age should I start using retinol or anti-aging products?

Most dermatologists recommend introducing retinol in the mid-to-late 20s for preventive anti-aging benefits. The key is to start with a low concentration (0.25% to 0.3%) twice per week and gradually increase frequency. Sunscreen is the most important anti-aging product at any age and should be used daily from adolescence onward.

Your Next Step

Start with the bare-faced test to identify your skin type before buying any new products. Once you know whether your skin is dry, oily, combination, sensitive, or normal, build your AM routine first — gentle cleanse, hydrating toner, antioxidant serum, moisturizer, and SPF. After you have the morning routine down consistently for two weeks, add the PM routine with double cleansing and a targeted treatment appropriate for your skin type.

The best routine is the one you can maintain every day. A simple three-step routine done consistently will outperform a ten-step routine you skip half the week. For more guidance, explore our guide to choosing the right cleanser for your skin and learn how morning and evening routines differ.
