5 Science-Backed Study Techniques for Faster Learning

June 1, 2026 8 min read

1. Retrieval Practice: The Power of Active Recall

Retrieval practice, often called the testing effect, is one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology. It involves actively bringing information to mind from memory rather than passively rereading notes or watching lectures. A landmark study by Roediger and Karpicke (2006) found that students who practiced retrieval after reading a passage remembered 50% more after one week compared to those who simply restudied the material.

Why does it work? Each time you retrieve a memory, you strengthen the neural pathways associated with that information. This process makes the memory more durable and easier to access in the future. For example, instead of highlighting a textbook chapter, close the book and try to recall the main points. Then check what you missed. This simple shift transforms passive review into an active learning session.

To implement retrieval practice effectively, use flashcards (digital or paper), write summaries from memory, or take practice tests without notes. Aim to retrieve information soon after learning and then again after a delay. Research shows that effortful retrieval -- even when you can't recall everything -- boosts long-term retention more than easy forms of review.

Key Stat: Students who used retrieval practice scored an average of 1.5 grade points higher on final exams than those who relied on rereading (McDaniel et al., 2007).

2. Spaced Repetition: Timing Your Reviews for Maximum Impact

Spaced repetition is the practice of reviewing material at increasing intervals over time. It directly counteracts the forgetting curve described by Hermann Ebbinghaus in the 1880s, which shows that we lose about 50% of new information within hours if we don't review it. By spacing out your study sessions, you force your brain to reconstruct memories repeatedly, deepening each recall.

Modern apps like Anki and Quizlet implement spaced repetition algorithmically, but you can create your own schedule. For instance, review a concept after one day, then three days, then one week, then one month. This approach is particularly effective for subjects that require memorization, such as vocabulary, anatomy, or historical timelines. A meta-analysis by Cepeda et al. (2006) found that spaced learning produced significantly better retention than massed (cramming) learning across all age groups.

To get started, break your study material into small chunks and schedule short sessions (20-30 minutes) spread over days. Avoid the temptation to review everything in one marathon session. Your brain needs time to consolidate information. Combining spaced repetition with retrieval practice -- testing yourself each time -- creates a powerful synergy that locks knowledge into long-term memory.

3. Interleaving: Mixing It Up for Deeper Understanding

Interleaving means switching between different topics or types of problems during a single study session, rather than mastering one concept before moving to the next (blocked practice). While blocked practice feels more productive because you perform well immediately, interleaving forces your brain to constantly identify which strategy to apply, building a more flexible and robust understanding.

Consider learning mathematics: instead of solving ten problems on the same formula, mix problems from different formulas. A study by Rohrer and Taylor (2007) showed that students who interleaved types of math problems scored 43% higher on a test one week later than those who used blocked practice. The same principle applies to learning languages, music, and even sports skills.

Interleaving can feel harder and more frustrating at first -- that's a good sign. The struggle to recall the correct approach strengthens discrimination between concepts. To implement, create a study schedule that rotates topics every 15-30 minutes. Use practice sets that randomly combine different problem types. Over time, you'll develop a deeper, more connected knowledge base that transfers better to real-world situations.

4. Elaboration: Connecting New Ideas to What You Already Know

Elaboration involves explaining new information in your own words and connecting it to existing knowledge. This technique deepens understanding by linking fresh concepts to your personal mental framework. When you elaborate, you ask questions like "Why does this make sense?" and "How is this similar to something I already know?"

For example, if you're learning about photosynthesis, you might elaborate by explaining the process to a friend or comparing it to how solar panels convert sunlight into energy. Research by Pressley et al. (1987) found that elaboration significantly improved recall for complex text passages. The more connections you create, the richer the memory trace becomes, making it easier to retrieve later.

To practice elaboration, try self-explanation: stop after reading a paragraph and explain the key ideas aloud or in writing without looking at the source. Use analogies and examples from your own life. Another effective method is the Feynman Technique -- teach a concept to someone else using simple language. If you get stuck, that reveals gaps in your understanding. Filling those gaps through elaboration solidifies learning.

5. Dual Coding: Combining Words and Visuals for Stronger Memory

Dual coding theory, proposed by Allan Paivio, suggests that our brains process verbal and visual information through two separate channels. When you combine text with relevant images, diagrams, or charts, you create two mental representations of the same information, which increases the chances of recalling it later. This is why infographics and illustrated textbooks are often more memorable than plain text.

For instance, instead of reading a description of the human circulatory system, a diagram of the heart with labels and arrows showing blood flow makes the concept easier to grasp and retain. A meta-analysis by Mayer (2009) found that students who learned with integrated text and graphics performed 89% better on transfer tests than those who learned from text alone. The key is that the visual must be directly relevant -- extraneous images can actually hurt learning.

To apply dual coding, create concept maps, draw flowcharts, or sketch simple diagrams that represent relationships between ideas. Use color coding to highlight categories. When studying from video lectures, pause and draw a visual summary. Even narration paired with simple sketches (as seen in many YouTube explainers) leverages dual coding. The combination of words and pictures keeps both brain channels engaged, reducing cognitive load and improving long-term retention.
