Workout Tracking Methods Compared: App vs Notebook vs Spreadsheet

9 min read May 31, 2026 Jordan Myers

Key Takeaways

  • Notebooks offer low cost and zero distractions but require manual calculation and have no backup.
  • Spreadsheets provide powerful data analysis and customization but need significant setup time and a device.
  • Fitness apps balance convenience and automation with features like rest timers and cloud sync, often at a monthly cost.
  • Long-term progress tracking is easiest with spreadsheets or apps that export data for trend analysis.
  • Choose a method based on your training level, need for data depth, and tolerance for setup time.

Why Tracking Your Workouts Matters

Consistent workout tracking is the single most reliable way to ensure progressive overload. Without a record of what you lifted last week, you risk stalling or overtraining. Studies show that individuals who log their training improve adherence by over 30% compared to those who don't. Tracking allows you to see exactly when to increase weight by 2.5 kg or add an extra rep to each set.

Three primary methods dominate the fitness world: a physical notebook, a dedicated fitness app, and a spreadsheet. Each has distinct strengths and weaknesses depending on your training style, tech comfort, and need for data analysis. This article compares them across five key criteria: ease of use, data depth, portability, long-term analysis, and cost.

Notebook: Simple and Uninterrupted

A notebook requires no battery, no Wi-Fi, and no app updates. You open it, write your warm-up sets of 20 kg for 3x12, then your working sets of 50 kg for 4x8. Many lifters find the physical act of writing reinforces memory and focus. You can also sketch exercise variations or note how a set felt without character limits.

The downside: no automatic calculations. If you want to know your total weekly volume (sets x reps x weight), you manually sum each entry. For a 5-day split with 15 sets per day, that's 75 calculations per week. Over a 12-week mesocycle, that becomes tedious. Also, notebooks are easy to misplace and don't back up. If you spill water on your gym bag, that month of data is gone.

For lifters who prefer minimalism and a distraction-free environment, a simple spiral notebook costing under $5 remains a top choice. It forces you to be present and deliberate.

Spreadsheet: Maximum Data Control

A spreadsheet, whether in Google Sheets or Excel, offers the most analytical power. You can create columns for date, exercise, weight, sets, reps, RPE, and notes. With formulas, you can automatically calculate volume load: for a squat session of 100 kg for 5x5, volume = 100 x 5 x 5 = 2500 kg. You can also track trend lines for your 1RM estimates over 6 to 12 months.

One major advantage: you can build custom dashboards. For example, you can create a sheet that shows your bench press progress over the last 16 weeks, including deload weeks. You can also share it with a coach who can leave feedback in a comments column.

The trade-off is setup time. Building a robust spreadsheet may take 2 to 3 hours initially. You also need a device to edit it — typically a laptop or tablet. While mobile apps exist, entering data on a phone screen during a workout is slower than writing in a notebook or tapping in a dedicated app. For data-driven athletes who love numbers, spreadsheets are unmatched.

Fitness Apps: Convenience and Automation

Fitness apps like Strong, Hevy, or JEFIT provide a middle ground. They offer pre-built templates for popular programs such as 5/3/1, Starting Strength, or PPL splits. You tap to log a set, and the app automatically calculates rest timers, volume, and estimated 1RM. For example, after logging 80 kg for 3 sets of 5 reps, the app might estimate your 1RM at 90 kg using the Epley formula.

Most apps sync to cloud storage, so your data is safe if you switch phones. They also include built-in timers, progress charts, and often a social feed for sharing PRs. The best apps let you export data to CSV, giving you spreadsheet-level analysis if desired.

However, apps can be distracting. Notification pings from other apps can break concentration. Many also require a subscription — $5 to $15 per month — for full features like custom routines or advanced analytics. For the average gym-goer who wants plug-and-play convenience and automatic progress tracking, a fitness app is the most efficient choice.

Comparing Long-Term Data Analysis

Long-term analysis separates serious athletes from casual trainees. After 6 months of training, you should be able to answer: Did my squat 1RM increase by at least 10%? Did my bench press volume plateau in weeks 8 through 12? Spreadsheets excel here — you can create a pivot table to compare volume by exercise across months. Apps often show basic line charts but may limit historical data to 12 months on free tiers.

Notebooks require manual review. You'd flip through pages to compare your squat numbers from January vs June. That's doable but time-consuming. A 6-month notebook with 180 entries might take 30 minutes to review manually.

If you're serious about periodization, you need a system that lets you look back at least 16 weeks of data to spot trends in fatigue and recovery. A spreadsheet gives you that power without the guesswork.

For most lifters, a combo approach works: use an app during workouts for quick logging, then export to a spreadsheet for monthly analysis. This gives you the best of both worlds.

Cost and Accessibility Considerations

Notebooks are the cheapest — a high-quality A5 grid notebook costs $8 to $15 and lasts 4 to 6 months. Spreadsheets are free if you use Google Sheets, but you need a device. A basic smartphone works, but comfortable data entry usually requires a laptop or tablet, which adds cost if you don't already own one.

Fitness apps range from free (limited features) to $10/month for premium. Over a year, that's $120. Some apps offer lifetime purchase options around $40 to $60. Compare that to a notebook at $20 per year and you see a clear price difference. However, the time saved in manual calculations and automatic rest timers may justify the subscription for time-pressed individuals.

Accessibility also matters: notebooks work anywhere, even in a power outage. Apps require a charged phone and often internet access. If you train in a basement gym with poor signal, offline-capable apps or a notebook become essential.

Practical Recommendation for Lifters

Choose based on your training goals and environment. For a beginner running a simple linear progression (e.g., add 2.5 kg each session), a notebook is perfect. Write down: date, exercise, weight, sets, reps. That's all you need for the first 8 to 12 weeks.

For intermediate to advanced lifters using periodized programs (e.g., 5/3/1 with joker sets, or a DUP template with varying intensities), an app or spreadsheet is better. The app handles complex rest timers and percentage calculations. For example, in week 3 of a 5/3/1 cycle, you need to calculate 85%, 90%, and 95% of your training max. An app does that instantly.

If you coach others or analyze your data deeply, invest time in a spreadsheet. It gives you the flexibility to track RPE, sleep, and nutrition alongside lifts. No single method is superior for everyone. Test each for two weeks and stick with the one that keeps you consistent.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about fitness tech and apps

What is the best workout tracking method for beginners?

A simple notebook is best for beginners. It forces you to focus on basic progressive overload without app distractions. Write down exercise, weight, sets, and reps each session.

Can I switch between tracking methods without losing data?

Yes, most apps allow CSV export, which you can import into a spreadsheet. Notebook data must be manually entered if you switch to digital. Plan a transition week to re-enter historical data.

How much time does spreadsheet setup take?

Initial setup takes 2 to 3 hours to build columns, formulas, and charts. After that, logging each workout takes about 1 to 2 minutes per session on a laptop. Pre-built templates reduce setup time.

Do fitness apps work offline?

Many fitness apps like Hevy and JEFIT offer offline mode for logging sets and reps. Syncing occurs when you reconnect to the internet. Check the app's features before subscribing if you train in a low-signal area.

Start Today

No single tracking method fits every lifter. A notebook gives simplicity and zero cost. A spreadsheet offers unmatched data analysis. A fitness app provides convenience and automation. The best choice is the one you will use consistently for at least 12 weeks. Start with the method that aligns with your current tech comfort and training complexity, then adjust as your needs evolve.
