Vitamin and Mineral Deficiencies: Signs and Solutions

RB

Dr. Rachel Brown, MD, RD

May 3, 2026 · 10 min read

Nutrient deficiencies are far more common than most people realize. In the United States, an estimated 30% of the population is deficient in at least one vitamin or mineral, and many people walk around with suboptimal levels that are not low enough to cause overt disease but are insufficient for optimal health. The symptoms can be subtle — fatigue, brain fog, brittle nails, frequent illness — and are often misattributed to stress, aging, or a busy lifestyle.

This guide covers the five most common nutrient deficiencies in developed countries: iron, vitamin D, vitamin B12, magnesium, and zinc. For each, you will learn the telltale signs your body may be signaling, the best food sources for repletion, when supplementation makes sense, and what blood tests to request from your doctor.

Key Concepts: Why Deficiencies Happen in Modern Diets

Advertisement

Even in countries with abundant food, nutrient deficiencies occur for several reasons. Modern agricultural practices have reduced the mineral content of soil, meaning today's vegetables may contain fewer micronutrients than those grown decades ago. Processed foods, which make up nearly 60% of the average American diet, are stripped of many nutrients during manufacturing and are often "enriched" with only a handful of vitamins rather than the full spectrum naturally present in whole foods. Certain life stages dramatically increase nutrient demands — pregnancy, breastfeeding, intense athletic training, and recovery from illness or surgery all raise requirements above typical intake levels. Medications can deplete nutrients: proton pump inhibitors (acid reflux drugs) impair B12 and magnesium absorption; metformin depletes B12; diuretics flush out magnesium and potassium. Finally, dietary restrictions — vegan, vegetarian, dairy-free, gluten-free — can eliminate major food sources of specific nutrients if not thoughtfully managed.

Deep Dive: The Five Most Common Deficiencies

Advertisement

1. Iron Deficiency: The Most Common Deficiency Worldwide

Iron is essential for producing hemoglobin, the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen throughout the body. When iron levels are inadequate, tissues receive insufficient oxygen — a condition called iron-deficiency anemia. This is the most common nutritional deficiency globally, affecting an estimated 2 billion people, with menstruating women at highest risk due to monthly blood loss.

Signs and symptoms to watch for: Persistent fatigue and weakness that sleep does not resolve; pale skin, especially noticeable in the inner lower eyelids and nail beds; shortness of breath with minimal exertion; cold hands and feet; brittle or spoon-shaped nails (koilonychia); unusual cravings for non-food items like ice, dirt, or starch — a condition called pica that is strongly associated with iron deficiency; restless leg syndrome, particularly at night; and frequent headaches or dizziness.

Best dietary sources: Heme iron (from animal sources) is absorbed 2-3 times more efficiently than non-heme iron. Top sources include beef liver (6.5mg per 3oz), oysters (8mg per 3oz), lean beef (2.5mg per 3oz), and dark turkey meat (2mg per 3oz). Non-heme iron sources include lentils (3.3mg per half-cup cooked), spinach (3.2mg per half-cup cooked), pumpkin seeds (2.5mg per ounce), and fortified cereals. Pair non-heme iron sources with vitamin C to dramatically increase absorption — a squeeze of lemon on spinach, or bell peppers alongside lentils. Avoid drinking tea or coffee with iron-rich meals, as tannins inhibit absorption.

When to supplement: Supplement only if blood tests confirm deficiency, as excess iron is pro-oxidative and can damage organs. Ferrous sulfate is the most common and affordable form, but it causes constipation in many people. Ferrous bisglycinate is gentler on the stomach and equally effective. Take iron supplements every other day rather than daily — research shows this improves absorption and reduces side effects. Typical dosing is 60-120mg of elemental iron per dose.

Relevant blood tests: Complete blood count (CBC) to check hemoglobin and hematocrit; serum ferritin (the most sensitive marker of iron stores — optimal is 50-100 ng/mL for women, 100-150 ng/mL for men, though reference ranges are wider); serum iron and total iron-binding capacity (TIBC); transferrin saturation.

2. Vitamin D Deficiency: The Sunshine Vitamin Crisis

Vitamin D functions more like a hormone than a vitamin, influencing over 200 genes, calcium absorption, immune function, and mood regulation. Unlike other vitamins, the primary source is not food but sunlight — UVB rays trigger vitamin D synthesis in the skin. Modern indoor lifestyles, sunscreen use, and northern latitudes have made deficiency extremely common. An estimated 40% of American adults have insufficient levels, rising to over 70% in some populations.

Signs and symptoms: Frequent illness — vitamin D is critical for immune defense, and low levels correlate with higher rates of respiratory infections. Bone and back pain — vitamin D enables calcium absorption; without it, bones can become painful and brittle. Depression or persistent low mood — vitamin D receptors exist throughout the brain in areas involved in mood regulation. Slow wound healing. Muscle weakness and unexplained muscle pain. Hair loss, particularly in women. Extreme fatigue.

Best dietary sources: Fatty fish: wild-caught salmon (600-1,000 IU per 3oz serving), sardines, mackerel, and tuna. Egg yolks (40 IU per yolk — modest). UV-exposed mushrooms. Fortified foods: milk, orange juice, and cereals that have vitamin D added. Note that diet alone rarely provides sufficient vitamin D — a serving of salmon might provide 600 IU, but optimal daily intake is 1,500-4,000 IU for most adults.

When to supplement: Most people living above the 37th parallel (roughly north of Los Angeles, Atlanta, or the Mediterranean) should consider supplementation from fall through spring, when UVB rays are insufficient for vitamin D synthesis. Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is superior to D2 (ergocalciferol) — it raises and maintains blood levels more effectively. Take with a meal containing fat, as vitamin D is fat-soluble and absorption increases significantly with dietary fat. Typical maintenance dose is 1,000-4,000 IU daily; therapeutic doses for deficiency are higher and should be medically supervised.

Relevant blood tests: 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] — this is the circulating form that reflects vitamin D status. Optimal levels are 40-60 ng/mL, though most labs flag deficiency below 20 ng/mL and insufficiency below 30 ng/mL. Some functional medicine practitioners target 50-80 ng/mL.

3. Vitamin B12 Deficiency: The Energy and Nerve Vitamin

Vitamin B12 is essential for red blood cell formation, neurological function, and DNA synthesis. Deficiency can cause irreversible nerve damage if left untreated for too long. Vegans and vegetarians are at highest risk because B12 is found almost exclusively in animal products. Older adults are also at elevated risk because stomach acid production declines with age, impairing B12 absorption from food. Additionally, the widespread use of acid-suppressing medications (PPIs like omeprazole) significantly increases B12 deficiency risk.

Signs and symptoms: Tingling, numbness, or burning sensations in hands and feet (peripheral neuropathy) — this is the hallmark neurological symptom and can become permanent. Memory problems and cognitive decline that can mimic early dementia. Smooth, red, swollen tongue (glossitis). Persistent fatigue and weakness. Difficulty with balance and coordination. Mood changes including depression and irritability. Pale or jaundiced skin.

Best dietary sources: Clams (84 mcg per 3oz — over 1,000% of daily needs), beef liver (70 mcg), sardines (8 mcg), beef (2.5 mcg), salmon (4.8 mcg), eggs (0.6 mcg per large egg), dairy products. Nutritional yeast fortified with B12 is the primary vegan source — check labels, as not all brands fortify. Most plant milks and breakfast cereals are fortified with B12.

When to supplement: All vegans should supplement B12. Adults over 50 and anyone on long-term acid-suppressing medication should consider supplementation. Cyanocobalamin is the most common and stable supplemental form; methylcobalamin is the active form that some prefer. Sublingual (under the tongue) tablets are popular, though evidence for superior absorption over standard oral supplements is mixed. For significant deficiency, especially with neurological symptoms, B12 injections bypass absorption issues entirely and are typically administered by a physician. Typical oral dose: 1,000-2,000 mcg daily for deficiency correction, then 1,000 mcg daily or 2,000 mcg weekly for maintenance.

Relevant blood tests: Serum B12 — levels below 200 pg/mL indicate deficiency; 200-400 pg/mL is a gray zone where some people are symptomatic. Methylmalonic acid (MMA) is a more sensitive marker — it rises when B12 is functionally insufficient, even if serum B12 appears normal. Homocysteine — elevated levels can indicate B12, folate, or B6 deficiency.

4. Magnesium Deficiency: The Overlooked Mineral

Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the body, including muscle and nerve function, blood glucose regulation, blood pressure control, and protein synthesis. Despite its importance, it is estimated that 50-60% of Americans do not meet the recommended daily intake. Magnesium is depleted by stress (cortisol increases magnesium excretion), alcohol consumption, certain medications, and the consumption of refined grains stripped of their magnesium-rich germ and bran.

Signs and symptoms: Muscle cramps, twitches, and spasms (especially at night or after exercise) — magnesium regulates muscle contraction and relaxation; deficiency causes excessive contraction. Poor sleep or insomnia — magnesium supports GABA, a neurotransmitter that promotes relaxation and sleep. Anxiety and irritability. Fatigue and weakness. Irregular heartbeat or heart palpitations. Migraines and tension headaches — magnesium deficiency is well-documented in migraine sufferers, and supplementation is a recognized preventive treatment. Constipation — magnesium draws water into the intestines and relaxes intestinal muscles.

Best dietary sources: Pumpkin seeds (156mg per ounce), almonds (80mg per ounce), spinach (78mg per half-cup cooked), black beans (60mg per half-cup), dark chocolate 70-85% cocoa (64mg per ounce), avocado (58mg per avocado), banana (32mg per medium banana). The magnesium content of foods has declined over time due to soil depletion — some estimates suggest a 20-30% decrease since the 1950s.

When to supplement: Consider supplementation if you have symptoms, a diet low in magnesium-rich foods, high stress levels, or heavy alcohol consumption. Magnesium glycinate is well-absorbed and less likely to cause digestive issues — it is the preferred form for addressing deficiency. Magnesium citrate is well-absorbed but has a pronounced laxative effect (useful if constipation is a concern). Magnesium oxide is inexpensive but poorly absorbed — avoid this form unless specifically using it as a laxative. Typical dose: 200-400mg of elemental magnesium daily, taken in divided doses or before bed. Start with a lower dose and increase gradually to assess digestive tolerance.

Relevant blood tests: Serum magnesium is the standard test but is a poor measure of total body magnesium status — only about 1% of the body's magnesium is in the blood. RBC (red blood cell) magnesium is more reflective of tissue levels. Many physicians diagnose magnesium deficiency based on symptoms and dietary assessment rather than blood tests, given the limitations of testing.

5. Zinc Deficiency: The Immune and Skin Mineral

Zinc is critical for immune function, wound healing, protein synthesis, and hundreds of enzymatic reactions. The body has no dedicated zinc storage system, so consistent dietary intake is necessary. Vegetarians, vegans, pregnant women, and the elderly are at increased risk. Zinc absorption is inhibited by phytates in whole grains and legumes — the same foods that are primary zinc sources for plant-based eaters.

Signs and symptoms: Frequent infections and slow wound healing — zinc is essential for immune cell development and function. Hair loss — zinc deficiency disrupts the hair growth cycle. White spots on fingernails (leukonychia) — a classic but not definitive sign. Loss of taste or smell. Diarrhea. Skin rashes, especially around the mouth, eyes, and extremities (acrodermatitis enteropathica in severe cases). Loss of appetite. Impaired growth and development in children.

Best dietary sources: Oysters (74mg per 3oz — by far the richest source, providing well over 500% of daily needs in a single serving), beef (5.3mg per 3oz), crab (6.5mg per 3oz), pumpkin seeds (2.2mg per ounce), chickpeas (1.3mg per half-cup), cashews (1.6mg per ounce). Soaking, sprouting, or fermenting grains and legumes reduces phytate content and improves zinc bioavailability.

When to supplement: Consider if you are vegetarian/vegan, have frequent infections, slow wound healing, or hair loss. Zinc picolinate and zinc gluconate are well-absorbed forms. Zinc oxide is less bioavailable. Long-term zinc supplementation can induce copper deficiency, as zinc and copper compete for absorption — supplements providing more than 40mg daily should include copper (typically in a 15:1 zinc-to-copper ratio). Typical dose: 15-30mg daily for general support; higher doses for therapeutic purposes should be time-limited and supervised. Take with food to reduce nausea, a common side effect.

Relevant blood tests: Serum or plasma zinc — levels below 70 mcg/dL suggest deficiency, though mild deficiency can exist at levels slightly higher. Like magnesium, blood levels do not perfectly reflect tissue status, and diagnosis is often clinical.

Practical Application: Creating Your Nutrient-Sufficiency Plan

Advertisement

A Food-First Approach

Supplements should supplement, not replace, a nutrient-dense diet. Whole foods provide nutrients in complexes with co-factors that enhance absorption and utilization — a synergy that isolated supplements cannot fully replicate. Before reaching for a pill bottle, audit your diet with an honest assessment. Are you eating leafy greens daily? Are you consuming a variety of protein sources? Do fermented foods appear regularly? Are whole grains and legumes on your plate most days? An eating pattern that answers yes to these questions resolves most deficiencies without supplementation.

When to Get Blood Work

Routine blood testing for nutrient levels is not always covered by insurance unless symptoms are present, but it is worth discussing with your doctor if you experience persistent unexplained fatigue, brain fog, mood disturbances, frequent illness, or any of the specific symptoms outlined above. Annual physicals typically include a CBC (which can flag iron-deficiency anemia) but not vitamin D, B12, magnesium, or zinc levels. You may need to specifically request these tests. Fasting morning blood draws provide the most accurate results for most nutrients.

Building Your Nutrient-Dense Plate

A practical checklist for each meal: Does the plate contain a source of protein (animal or plant)? Is there at least one serving of vegetables, ideally two? Is there a source of healthy fat to enable absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)? Are whole foods prioritized over processed ones? Is there variety from day to day — different vegetables, different proteins, different grains? Consistency matters more than perfection. A single nutrient-dense meal does not correct a deficiency, but a pattern of nutrient-dense meals, sustained over weeks and months, does.

A Note on Self-Diagnosis: The symptoms described above can have multiple causes. Fatigue could be iron deficiency, B12 deficiency, thyroid dysfunction, sleep apnea, or depression. Use this guide to have an informed conversation with your healthcare provider, not to self-diagnose in isolation. When in doubt, test — do not guess.
