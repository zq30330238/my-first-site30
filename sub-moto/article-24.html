Tailing TL800 Review: Long-Range Electric Scooter Redefining Daily Commuting

May 24, 2026 8 min read

Real-World Range Performance: City Streets and Suburban Roads

The Tailing TL800's headline claim of 80 miles (128 km) per charge is impressive on paper, but real-world conditions tell a more nuanced story. We tested the TL800 over a week on a mixed route: 60% city streets with stop-and-go traffic and 40% suburban roads with steady 35-45 mph speeds. The scooter's 72V 32Ah lithium-ion battery pack, using high-density 21700 cells, delivered an average of 68 miles (109 km) before the battery indicator dropped to 10%. That's a 15% reduction from the advertised range, which is typical for electric scooters in this class. On purely city routes with frequent braking and acceleration, range dropped to 62 miles (100 km), while steady suburban cruising pushed it to 74 miles (119 km). The regenerative braking system recovers an estimated 8-10% of energy during deceleration, adding roughly 5-7 miles per charge in stop-and-go conditions. For daily commuters covering 20-30 miles round trip, the TL800 easily handles two days of riding without a recharge. The battery charges from empty to 80% in 4.5 hours using the standard 2A charger, or 6.5 hours for a full charge. A fast charger option (5A) cuts that to 2.5 hours for 80%, but it's an aftermarket accessory.

Key Stat: In our real-world testing, the Tailing TL800 averaged 68 miles per charge--enough for a 30-mile round-trip commute with over a day of buffer remaining.

Powertrain: Hub Motor Specs, Battery Chemistry, and Regenerative Braking

The TL800 is powered by a rear-mounted 1,200W continuous-rated hub motor, peaking at 2,400W for short bursts during acceleration or hill climbs. This motor delivers 110 Nm of torque at the wheel, which translates to brisk acceleration from a standstill--0 to 20 mph in 4.2 seconds--and a top speed of 45 mph (72 km/h) on flat ground. The motor's efficiency curve peaks at around 25-30 mph, where it draws roughly 800W, making that the sweet spot for maximizing range. The battery uses lithium iron phosphate (LiFePO4) chemistry, which offers over 2,000 charge cycles before capacity drops to 80%, compared to 500-800 cycles for standard lithium-ion. This means the TL800's battery should last 5-7 years under daily commuting use. The regenerative braking system is adjustable via the TFT display: you can set it to low, medium, or high regen. On the high setting, the motor provides noticeable deceleration when you release the throttle, similar to engine braking on a gas scooter. In our tests, high regen recovered about 10% of energy on a 15-mile city route, while low regen recovered only 3%. The trade-off is that high regen makes the throttle feel less smooth, so most riders will prefer medium for daily use. The motor controller uses a sine wave design, which reduces audible whine and provides smoother power delivery compared to square wave controllers found on cheaper scooters.

Comfort Features: Suspension Travel, Seat Design, and Ergonomics for Tall Riders

Comfort is a standout feature of the TL800, especially for taller riders. The scooter uses a front telescopic fork with 3.5 inches (89 mm) of travel and a rear dual shock setup with 3.0 inches (76 mm) of travel, both preload-adjustable. On potholed city streets, the suspension absorbs bumps well, though sharp edges like speed bumps at speed can bottom out the rear if the preload is set too soft. We recommend setting the rear preload to the middle position for riders 160-200 lbs, and firm for heavier riders. The seat is 32 inches (813 mm) long and 8 inches (203 mm) wide, with a stepped design that provides a natural hip stop during acceleration. The foam density is medium-firm, offering support without being hard. For riders 5'10" to 6'3", the seat height of 30.5 inches (775 mm) allows a flat-footed stance at stops, while the handlebars are positioned 4 inches higher than the seat, creating a relaxed upright posture. The floorboard is 16 inches (406 mm) long, giving tall riders enough room to shift foot positions during long rides. The windscreen is adjustable by 2 inches via a tool-less mechanism, directing airflow over the helmet at speeds above 35 mph. After a 45-mile continuous ride, we experienced minimal fatigue in the lower back and wrists, which is rare for scooters in this price range. The TL800 also includes a padded backrest for the passenger, though it's not adjustable.

Tailing's Brand Positioning: Competing with Yadea and Aima on Value and Range

Tailing is a relatively new name in the global electric scooter market, but it has quickly carved out a niche by focusing on range and value. The TL800 is priced at $2,499, which undercuts Yadea's G5S ($2,999) and Aima's M1 ($2,799) while offering comparable or better range. Yadea's G5S claims 75 miles (121 km) but delivered 62 miles (100 km) in our tests, while the Aima M1 claims 70 miles (113 km) and delivered 58 miles (93 km). The TL800's 68-mile real-world range gives it a clear advantage for commuters who need to go farther between charges. Tailing's build quality is also competitive: the frame uses a high-strength steel alloy with a powder-coated finish that resists corrosion, and the plastic body panels have tight gaps and no rattles. However, Tailing's dealer network is smaller than Yadea's or Aima's, which means parts availability and service support may be limited in some regions. The company offers a 2-year warranty on the battery and motor, matching Yadea's coverage, but Aima offers 3 years on the battery. Tailing's app connectivity is basic compared to Yadea's G5S, which has GPS tracking, remote lock, and ride statistics. The TL800's app only shows battery level, estimated range, and trip distance. For riders who prioritize range and value over smart features, the TL800 is a compelling choice. Tailing's strategy is clear: deliver the longest real-world range at the lowest price, and let the numbers speak for themselves. In a market where Yadea and Aima have brand recognition, Tailing is betting that performance will win over buyers.

Verdict: Should You Buy the Tailing TL800?

The Tailing TL800 is an excellent choice for daily commuters who need maximum range without breaking the bank. Its real-world range of 68 miles is class-leading, the suspension and seat comfort are impressive for the price, and the LiFePO4 battery offers long-term durability. The main trade-offs are the basic app features, smaller dealer network, and slightly slower charging compared to some competitors. If you value range above all else and don't need advanced connectivity, the TL800 is the best value in its segment. For riders who want GPS tracking, remote features, or a larger service network, the Yadea G5S or Aima M1 may be better options, but you'll pay more for less range. The TL800 redefines what a long-range electric scooter can be at this price point, and it's a strong contender for anyone looking to switch from a gas scooter to electric commuting.
