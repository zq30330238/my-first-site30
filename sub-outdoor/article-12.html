Best Hiking Destinations: Top Trails Across Every Skill Level

Explore the best hiking destinations across America. Curated trail recommendations for beginners, intermediate, and advanced hikers in every region.

Home / Categories / Hiking Guide / Best Hiking Destinations: Top Trails Across Every Skill Level

America spans nearly every climate and terrain type on Earth, offering hiking opportunities that range from coastal boardwalks to alpine ridgelines. Whether you are lacing up your boots for the first time or planning your next thru-hike, the perfect trail awaits. This guide organizes the best hiking destinations by region and difficulty level so you can find your next adventure.

Key Takeaways

  • Each region offers unique hiking experiences. Match your trail choice to the season and your skill level.
  • National parks provide world-class hiking but require advance planning for permits and parking.
  • Beginner trails under 5 miles with minimal elevation gain exist in every region of the country.
  • Advanced hikers should research permit requirements, as many premier trails have quota systems.

Northeast Hiking Destinations

The Northeast offers a dense network of trails through ancient mountains and colorful forests. Acadia National Park in Maine provides coastal hiking with moderate elevation and stunning ocean views. The Precipice Trail offers advanced hikers a non-technical climbing experience with iron rungs and exposed ledges, while the Ocean Path provides beginners with a flat 4.4-mile coastal walk. New Hampshire's White Mountains challenge experienced hikers with the Presidential Traverse, an 23-mile ridge walk above treeline that tests navigation skills and weather judgment. The Appalachian Trail through Vermont and Massachusetts offers well-maintained sections suitable for all skill levels, with frequent road crossings that allow flexible trip planning. The Adirondacks in New York feature over 2,000 miles of trails ranging from gentle nature walks around Lake Placid to challenging ascents of High Peaks like Mount Marcy.

Southeast Hiking Destinations

The Southeast combines lush forests, dramatic gorges, and ancient mountains. Great Smoky Mountains National Park offers the widest variety of hiking in the region, with over 800 miles of trails ranging from the easy 2-mile Laurel Falls Trail to the challenging 11-mile Mount LeConte ascent. Shenandoah National Park in Virginia features 500 miles of trails along the Blue Ridge Mountains, with the Appalachian Trail running the entire 105-mile length of the park. The park's easy trails like Stony Man Nature Trail provide panoramic views with minimal effort. North Carolina's Linville Gorge challenges experienced hikers with rugged terrain and limited trail markings in one of the East's deepest canyons. Georgia's Blood Mountain on the Appalachian Trail offers a rewarding 4.4-mile round trip with 1,500 feet of elevation gain and panoramic views from the southern terminus of the AT approach.

West Coast Hiking Destinations

The West Coast offers unparalleled diversity from desert canyons to coastal bluffs to alpine peaks. Yosemite National Park in California features iconic trails like the Mist Trail to Vernal and Nevada Falls, a challenging but rewarding 6.8-mile round trip. The John Muir Trail represents the ultimate California hiking experience, running 211 miles through the Sierra Nevada. Olympic National Park in Washington offers three distinct ecosystems in one park: coastal beaches, temperate rainforest, and alpine peaks. The Hoh River Trail provides moderate hiking through moss-draped rainforest to mountain views. Oregon's Columbia River Gorge features dozens of waterfalls accessible by trails of varying difficulty, with the 2.6-mile Eagle Creek Trail offering a moderate hike past multiple waterfalls to Tunnel Falls.

Mountain West and Southwest Hiking Destinations

The Mountain West and Southwest feature the most dramatic landscapes in the country. Rocky Mountain National Park in Colorado offers over 350 miles of trails through alpine tundra and subalpine forests. The Dream Lake Trail provides an easy 2.2-mile round trip to an iconic alpine lake. The challenging Keyhole Route on Longs Peak requires route-finding skills and early starts to avoid afternoon thunderstorms. Glacier National Park in Montana features the Highline Trail, a world-renowned 11.8-mile one-way hike along the Continental Divide with exposure that challenges intermediate hikers. Utah's Zion National Park offers the unique Narrows hike, wading through the Virgin River between towering canyon walls. Arizona's Grand Canyon provides rim trails for beginners and challenging rim-to-rim crossings for experienced hikers prepared for significant elevation changes and extreme heat.

The best hiking destination is not the one with the most famous name or the highest elevation gain. It is the trail that matches your current fitness, experience level, and schedule. A perfect day on an easy trail beats a miserable day on a difficult one every time.

National park popularity has exploded in recent years. Popular trailhead parking fills by 7 or 8 AM during peak season. Plan to arrive early, use shuttle systems where available, and consider visiting less famous parks to escape the crowds while still experiencing incredible landscapes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which region is best for beginner hikers?

The Northeast and Southeast offer the most beginner-friendly trails with gentle grades, well-marked paths, and frequent road access. Shenandoah National Park, the Great Smoky Mountains, and Acadia National Park all provide excellent entry-level hiking with stunning scenery and good infrastructure.

When is the best time to hike in each region?

Northeast and Mountain West: June through September for most high-elevation trails. Southeast: October through May for comfortable temperatures. Southwest: March through May and September through November to avoid extreme heat. West Coast: Year-round depending on elevation, with summer being most reliable for alpine trails.

Do I need permits for these trails?

Many popular trails in national parks and wilderness areas require permits for overnight trips. Day hiking typically does not require permits, though some parks require timed entry reservations during peak season. Research permit requirements well in advance, as popular itineraries book months ahead.

Which destination offers the most solitude?

Less-visited national parks like Great Basin in Nevada, North Cascades in Washington, and Black Canyon of the Gunnison in Colorado offer spectacular hiking with significantly fewer crowds than flagship parks. National forests and BLM lands provide ample opportunities for solitude outside national park boundaries.
