What Is Ultra Running?

Ultra running is defined as any footrace longer than the standard marathon distance of 26.2 miles. Common ultra distances include 50 kilometers (31 miles), 50 miles, 100 kilometers (62 miles), and 100 miles. Ultra marathons are almost exclusively held on trails, though road ultras exist. The sport has experienced explosive growth, with participation increasing over 1,000% in the past two decades.

Ultra running differs from shorter distance running in fundamental ways. Pace becomes less important than persistence. Aid stations replace water fountains and gel packs. Night running becomes a necessary skill. The mental challenge of spending hours or days moving forward becomes as significant as the physical challenge.

An ultra marathon is not a longer race. It is a different sport. The first 26.2 miles are just the warmup. Everything beyond that is a conversation between your will and your doubts, and your will must win.

Is Ultra Running Right for You?

Before committing to ultra training, assess your baseline fitness and running history. Most successful ultra runners have at least 2-3 years of consistent running experience and have completed multiple half marathons or marathons. While it is possible to start with less running background, the injury risk increases significantly without a solid running foundation.

Time commitment is the most significant factor. Ultra training typically requires 6-10 hours per week during peak training, including weekend long runs taking 3-6 hours. This demands support from family and flexibility in work schedules. If you can commit the time and have the running foundation, ultra running offers some of the most rewarding experiences in endurance sports.

Training for Your First Ultra

A typical first-ultra training plan spans 16-24 weeks. The cornerstone is the weekly long run, which progressively increases to 20-30 miles by peak training. Long runs should be run on terrain similar to your target race, including comparable elevation gain. Back-to-back long runs on Saturday and Sunday simulate running on tired legs.

Weekly mileage typically peaks at 40-60 miles for a 50K, 50-70 miles for a 50-mile, and 60-80 miles for a 100K. Incorporate hill training, strength work, and recovery runs into your weekly schedule. Most importantly, practice your nutrition and hydration strategy during long training runs rather than experimenting on race day.

Ultra Running Gear Essentials

Ultra running requires gear beyond standard trail running equipment. A hydration vest with front flasks and rear storage is essential for carrying water, nutrition, and mandatory gear. Headlamps with extra batteries are required for races that extend into darkness. An emergency blanket or bivvy, whistle, and first aid kit are mandatory in most ultras.

Poles have become increasingly popular in ultra running for taking load off legs on steep climbs. Some races allow poles while others restrict them on certain sections. Invest in quality running socks and anti-chafing products, as the extended duration of ultra events makes comfort management critical. Test all gear during training before race day.

Nothing new on race day. This is the golden rule of ultra running. Every piece of gear, every food item, every clothing layer must be tested during training. Race day is for execution, not experimentation. A blister from untested socks or an upset stomach from an untried gel can end your race.

Nutrition and Hydration for Ultra Distances

Ultra nutrition differs from shorter races in both volume and variety. Aim for 200-300 calories per hour during the race, combining liquid calories from sports drinks with solid food. Many ultra runners find they crave savory food after several hours of sweet gels and drinks. Potato chips, quesadillas, broth, and watermelon are popular aid station fare.

Hydration requirements vary significantly based on temperature, effort, and individual sweat rates. A good rule is to drink to thirst and monitor urine color as a hydration indicator. Weigh yourself during training runs to understand your fluid needs. Overhydration (hyponatremia) is a real danger in ultras, especially in slower runners who may drink excessively.

Mental Strategies for Ultra Success

The mental challenge of ultra running often exceeds the physical challenge. Breaking the race into segments rather than focusing on the total distance makes the task manageable. Focus on reaching the next aid station rather than the finish line. Practice positive self-talk and have a mental playlist of songs, mantras, or memories to draw on during low points.

Low points are inevitable in ultra running. Every runner experiences them regardless of fitness level. The key is knowing that low points pass. Embrace the difficulty as part of the experience. Many ultra runners describe a profound sense of accomplishment and clarity that emerges during the hardest sections of a race, a state often called the flow state.

For a first 50K, consider the McKenzie River Trail Run in Oregon (50K, 3,200 feet of gain) or the Gorge Waterfalls 50K in Oregon’s Columbia River Gorge (7,500 feet of gain). Both offer well-marked courses, robust aid stations every 4–7 miles, and a supportive community atmosphere. For a 50-miler, the JFK 50 Mile in Maryland (historic C&O Canal towpath and Appalachian Trail) or the Bandera 50K in Texas (rocky, technical terrain with 4,500 feet of gain) provide excellent entry points. Always check the race’s cutoff times and mandatory gear lists—many first-time ultras require a headlamp, a hydration bladder of at least 1.5 liters, and an emergency blanket, as seen in the Leadville Trail 100’s mandatory gear list.

Gear selection directly impacts performance and comfort. The Salomon Adv Skin 12 hydration vest (with two 500ml soft flasks and a rear bladder compartment) is a top choice for its secure fit and multiple pockets for gels and salt tablets. For headlamps, the Petzl Swift RL (900 lumens, reactive lighting) or Black Diamond Spot 400 (400 lumens, IPX8 waterproof) provide reliable illumination for night sections. On technical trails, Hoka Speedgoat 5 shoes (with Vibram Megagrip outsole and 5mm lugs) offer superior traction on loose rock and mud, while the La Sportiva Bushido II (with a more aggressive tread and rock plate) excels on steep, rocky descents. Test all footwear on your training routes—blisters from untested shoes can sideline a 50-mile effort by mile 30.

Pacing and power hiking are critical techniques for ultras. On climbs exceeding 8–10% grade, power hiking conserves energy while maintaining forward progress—aim for a cadence of 100–120 steps per minute with hands on thighs for leverage. On flat sections, maintain a steady effort at a conversational pace (heart rate zone 2–3). For a 50K, target a 10–15% slower pace than your marathon pace, adjusting for elevation gain. Use a GPS watch like the Coros Apex 2 (with 45-hour battery life in standard mode) or Garmin Fenix 7X (with solar charging for extended runs) to monitor pace, distance, and elevation. Practice this pacing strategy on long runs at local trails like the Mount Si trail in Washington (8 miles, 3,150 feet gain) or the Runyon Canyon loop in Los Angeles (3.3 miles, 1,000 feet gain) to build race-specific endurance.

During a training run on the Pacific Crest Trail near Mount Whitney, runners can practice the critical skill of power hiking steep grades above 10,000 feet elevation. The HOKA Speedgoat 5 trail shoe provides essential traction on loose granite scree with its Vibram Megagrip outsole. Incorporating 30-second power hike intervals at a 15 percent grade builds the stabilizing muscles in the glutes and calves. This technique prevents early quad fatigue during 50-mile races like the Javelina Jundred in Arizona's Sonoran Desert.

Aid station strategy transforms at distances beyond 50 kilometers, requiring precise calorie timing from brands like Tailwind Nutrition and GU Energy Labs. For a 100-kilometer race such as the Leadville Trail Run in Colorado's San Isabel National Forest, runners should consume 200-300 calories per hour starting at mile 5. The Salomon ADV Skin 12 vest holds 1.5 liters of water while keeping gels and electrolyte tablets accessible in front pockets. Practicing this nutrition cadence during long training runs prevents the gastrointestinal distress that sidelines 40 percent of first-time ultra participants.

Night running demands specific gear like the Petzl Swift RL headlamp delivering 1100 lumens for navigating technical sections in the dark. On the Rocky Raccoon 100-mile course in Huntsville State Park, Texas, runners encounter root-laced singletrack that requires careful foot placement after sunset. Training runs should include simulated night sections using a headlamp on familiar trails to build proprioception and confidence. Carrying a backup light source, such as the Black Diamond Spot 400, ensures safety when batteries deplete during the overnight hours common in 100-mile events.
