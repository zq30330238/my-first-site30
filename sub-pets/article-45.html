Bearded Dragon Diet: Complete Food List for Every Life Stage

May 22, 2026 6 min read

Introduction

A bearded dragon's nutritional needs change dramatically from hatchling to adulthood. Feeding the wrong ratio of insects to plants--or offering inappropriate items--can lead to metabolic bone disease, obesity, or stunted growth. This guide provides a research-backed food list for each life stage, along with supplement schedules and foods to avoid. All recommendations are based on current herpetological guidelines from the Association of Reptilian and Amphibian Veterinarians.

Baby Bearded Dragons (0-4 Months)

Baby bearded dragons grow rapidly and require high protein to support bone and muscle development. The diet should consist of approximately 80% live insects and 20% finely chopped leafy greens. Offer insects that are no larger than the space between your bearded dragon's eyes to prevent impaction.

Safe Insects for Babies

  • Crickets (gut-loaded and dusted with calcium)
  • Dubia roaches (excellent calcium-to-phosphorus ratio)
  • Black soldier fly larvae (high calcium, low fat)
  • Small silkworms (nutrient-rich treat)

Avoid mealworms for babies--they have a hard exoskeleton and low nutritional value. Feed babies 15-25 small insects twice daily. Remove any uneaten insects after 10 minutes to prevent injury.

Vegetables for Babies

  • Collard greens (staple)
  • Mustard greens
  • Endive
  • Butternut squash (finely grated)

Chop greens into pieces smaller than the eye. Offer fresh greens daily and remove leftovers after 4 hours to avoid spoilage.

Key Stat: A 2023 study in the Journal of Herpetological Medicine found that bearded dragons fed a calcium-to-phosphorus ratio of 2:1 had 40% fewer instances of metabolic bone disease compared to those on a 1:1 ratio.

Juvenile Bearded Dragons (4-12 Months)

Juveniles are still growing but at a slower pace. Transition to a ratio of 60% insects, 40% vegetables. Offer larger insects like medium dubia roaches, superworms (sparingly), and hornworms. Reduce feeding frequency to once or twice a day, with 10-15 insects per feeding.

Expanded Vegetable List

  • Turnip greens
  • Dandelion greens (pesticide-free)
  • Yellow squash
  • Bell peppers (thinly sliced)
  • Green beans (steamed, chopped)

Juveniles can begin enjoying small amounts of fruit once or twice per week: blueberries, raspberries, or small pieces of papaya. Avoid citrus fruits due to high acidity.

Adult Bearded Dragons (12+ Months)

Adult bearded dragons are primarily herbivorous--80% vegetables and 20% insects. Overfeeding protein can cause kidney strain and obesity. Offer insects only 2-3 times per week, with 5-10 insects per feeding.

Preferred Vegetables for Adults

  • Collard greens (daily staple)
  • Escarole
  • Arugula (not too much--moderate oxalates)
  • Butternut squash (cooked, cubed)
  • Shredded carrots

Adults also benefit from occasional fruit: a few slices of strawberry or a grape quartered. Avoid banana and mango except as rare treats due to sugar content.

Foods to Avoid at All Life Stages

  • Avocado (persin is toxic)
  • Rhubarb (high oxalates)
  • Spinach (bind calcium in the gut)
  • Iceberg lettuce (no nutritional value)
  • Fireflies (toxic)
  • Wild-caught insects (pesticide risk)
  • Beetles with hard shells (impaction risk)

Essential Supplementation

Calcium and vitamin D3 are critical for bone health. Use a calcium powder with D3 for babies and juveniles at every insect feeding. Adults need calcium without D3 at most feedings, and with D3 twice per week. A multivitamin powder should be used once a week for all ages.

Research from the Journal of Zoo and Wildlife Medicine indicates that dusting insects just before feeding preserves nutrient content; pre-dusted insects lose potency within a few minutes.

Sample Feeding Schedule

Baby (0-4 months)

  • Morning: 15-20 small crickets dusted with calcium + D3
  • Afternoon: fresh chopped collard greens
  • Evening: 15-20 small dubia roaches dusted with multivitamin (once weekly)

Juvenile (4-12 months)

  • Morning: 10-15 medium insects with calcium + D3
  • Evening: salad of greens and squash

Adult (12+ months)

  • Daily: large salad of collard greens, squash, and bell pepper
  • Monday/Wednesday/Friday: 5-8 insects with calcium (no D3)
  • Once weekly: multivitamin dusting on salad

Consistency is key. Bearded dragons thrive on routine. Adjust portion sizes based on body condition score: a visible fat pad above the eyes indicates healthy weight; bony hips suggest underfeeding.

By following this life stage food list and supplement protocol, you can support your bearded dragon's growth, longevity, and vitality.
