Hedgehog Care: What You Need to Know Before Bringing One Home

May 22, 2026 7 min read

Hedgehogs have become increasingly popular as exotic pets, and it's easy to see why. With their curious snouts, adorable grunts, and spunky personalities, they make charming companions. However, hedgehogs have very specific care requirements that set them apart from more traditional pets. Before you bring one home, it's crucial to understand their needs to ensure a happy, healthy life for your new friend.

Is a Hedgehog Right for You?

Hedgehogs are nocturnal, solitary animals that require a dedicated owner. They are not cuddly pets like cats or dogs; they need time to bond and may never enjoy being held for long periods. Consider your lifestyle: hedgehogs need daily attention, a consistent environment, and specialized veterinary care, which can be expensive and hard to find.

According to the Hedgehog Welfare Society, nearly 70% of pet hedgehogs are surrendered within the first year due to unmet care expectations. Proper preparation is key to avoiding this statistic.

Setting Up the Perfect Home

Choosing a Cage

The minimum recommended cage size for a single hedgehog is 4 square feet (e.g., 2x2 feet), but larger is always better. A solid-bottom cage with smooth walls is ideal to prevent climbing injuries. Avoid wire-bottom cages, which can hurt their feet. Popular options include large plastic bins, C&C cages, or specialized hedgehog habitats.

Temperature and Lighting

Hedgehogs are sensitive to temperature. The ambient temperature should be between 72-80°F (22-27°C). If it drops below 70°F, they may attempt to hibernate, which can be fatal for captive hedgehogs. Use a ceramic heat emitter or a space heater with a thermostat. Provide a consistent day/night cycle with 12-14 hours of light; avoid direct sunlight.

Bedding and Accessories

Use paper-based bedding or fleece liners. Avoid cedar or pine shavings, as the oils can cause respiratory issues. Provide a hideout (like a igloo or fleece tunnel), a solid exercise wheel (at least 12 inches in diameter), and shallow food and water bowls. Hedgehogs also enjoy small toys, but ensure no small parts can be swallowed.

Feeding Your Hedgehog

A Balanced Diet

A high-quality cat food (chicken-based, low fat) is the staple for most hedgehogs. Look for a brand with at least 30% protein and under 15% fat. Supplement with insects like mealworms or crickets (2-3 times a week) and small amounts of fruits or vegetables (e.g., apple, carrot). Avoid dairy, nuts, seeds, and sugary foods.

Dr. Karen Rosenthal, a specialist in exotic pets, advises: 'A hedgehog's diet should mimic their natural insectivore diet. Too much fat can lead to obesity and liver disease.'

Feeding Schedule

Feed your hedgehog once daily in the evening, since they are nocturnal. Remove uneaten food in the morning. Always provide fresh water in a bowl (not a bottle, as they may not figure out how to use it).

Health and Veterinary Care

Hedgehogs are prone to several health issues: obesity, dental disease, skin mites, and wobbly hedgehog syndrome (a neurological condition). Find a vet experienced with hedgehogs before you get your pet. Schedule an initial wellness check within the first week.

Signs of a Healthy Hedgehog

  • Active and curious at night
  • Clear eyes and nose
  • Clean ears without discharge
  • Consistent eating and drinking
  • Firm, dark brown stool

Handling and Bonding

Patience is essential. Start by letting your hedgehog get used to your scent by placing an unwashed t-shirt in their cage. Handle them gently for short periods each day, preferably during their active hours (evening). Never force handling if they are balled up; instead, let them unroll naturally. Use a flat hand to scoop them from underneath.

Bathing and Nail Trimming

Most hedgehogs need a bath every few weeks using a mild, unscented shampoo made for small animals. Fill a sink with warm water to chest level. For nail trimming, use small animal nail clippers and have styptic powder on hand in case you cut the quick.

Legal Considerations

Check local laws: hedgehogs are illegal in some states (e.g., California, Georgia, Hawaii) and municipalities. Ensure you have the proper permits if required. It's also wise to adopt from a reputable breeder or rescue to avoid health issues.

Final Thoughts

Hedgehogs can be delightful, quirky pets that bring joy to the right owner. They require a significant commitment of time, money, and research. By understanding their unique needs—from temperature control to specialized diet—you can provide a loving home for your spiky companion.
