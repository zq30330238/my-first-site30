Pet Arthritis in Dogs and Cats: Signs, Treatment, and Home Relief Options

May 26, 2026 8 min read

What Are the Early Signs of Arthritis in Dogs and Cats?

Arthritis, or osteoarthritis, is a degenerative joint disease that affects millions of pets. In dogs, early signs often include stiffness after resting, a hesitation to climb stairs, or a subtle limp that comes and goes. Cats are masters at hiding pain, so watch for changes like reduced jumping onto counters, less grooming, or irritability when touched. Many owners first notice their pet 'slowing down' with age, but that slowdown could be arthritis pain. Other red flags include muscle wasting over the back legs, a bunny-hopping gait in dogs, or a cat that suddenly misses the litter box because squatting hurts.

According to the Arthritis Foundation, one in five dogs and one in three cats suffer from arthritis, yet many cases go undiagnosed because pets hide their pain so well.

If you see any of these signs, especially in a middle-aged or senior pet, schedule a veterinary exam. Early intervention can slow progression and improve quality of life dramatically. Remember that arthritis is painful--your pet isn't just 'getting old,' they are likely uncomfortable.

How Is Arthritis Diagnosed by a Veterinarian?

Your vet will start with a thorough physical exam, feeling each joint for swelling, heat, or crepitus (grating sensation). They will also watch your pet walk to identify lameness. X-rays are the gold standard for confirming arthritis--they reveal joint space narrowing, bone spurs, and thickened joint capsules. In some cases, advanced imaging like CT or MRI may be used for hips, elbows, or knees. Joint fluid analysis can rule out infection or immune-mediated disease. Blood work may be done to ensure your pet is a safe candidate for anti-inflammatory medications. Diagnosis is straightforward in most cases, but the key is catching it early--many pets are already in moderate to severe stages by the time owners bring them in.

What Medical Treatments Are Available for Pets with Arthritis?

Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are the cornerstone of medical management for arthritis pain in dogs. Carprofen, meloxicam, and deracoxib are common options that reduce inflammation and pain. For cats, NSAIDs are used more cautiously, often with buprenorphine or gabapentin for pain. Polysulfated glycosaminoglycan injections (Adequan) can help protect joint cartilage and are FDA-approved for both dogs and cats. In severe cases, corticosteroid injections may be considered, but they carry more side effects. Joint fluid modifiers like hyaluronic acid can also be injected directly into affected joints. For pets who don't respond to oral meds, surgery such as joint replacement, arthrodesis (fusion), or amputation may be discussed. Laser therapy and acupuncture are increasingly offered by veterinary rehab centers as drug-free adjuncts. Always consult your vet before giving any human pain reliever--acetaminophen and ibuprofen are toxic to pets.

Can Diet and Supplements Help Manage Pet Arthritis?

Absolutely. Weight management is the single most impactful non-drug treatment. Every extra pound adds stress on arthritic joints. Therapeutic diets like Hill's Prescription Diet j/d or Royal Canin Mobility Support are formulated with high omega-3 fatty acids (EPA), glucosamine, and chondroitin to reduce inflammation and support cartilage. Over-the-counter joint supplements vary widely in quality; look for brands that contain pharmaceutical-grade glucosamine hydrochloride, chondroitin sulfate, and green-lipped mussel extract. Omega-3 supplements (fish oil) are well-researched for reducing inflammatory mediators. CBD oil for pets is popular, but evidence is still emerging--talk to your vet about dosing and legality. While supplements are not cures, they can lower the dosage of pain meds needed and improve comfort. Allow 4-8 weeks to see benefits, and always choose products tested by third-party labs.

What Home Modifications Can Ease Arthritis Pain in Pets?

Small changes at home can make a huge difference. Provide orthopedic memory foam beds in warm, draft-free areas. Place ramps or stairs next to sofas and beds so your pet doesn't have to jump. Non-slip flooring (yoga mats, carpet runners, or booties) prevents painful slips on hardwood or tile. Keep food and water bowls elevated to reduce neck and back strain. For cats, place litter boxes on the same floor as the cat's main living area--avoid basement or top-floor trips. Warm compresses or a heating pad (on low, with a towel barrier) for 15 minutes can soothe sore joints. Gentle exercise like short, frequent walks or swimming maintains muscle strength without pounding joints. Physical therapy, including range-of-motion exercises and massage, can be taught by a veterinary rehabilitation therapist. Avoid high-impact activities like fetch on concrete or long hikes until pain is controlled.

How Can You Keep Your Arthritic Pet Comfortable and Happy Long-Term?

Think of arthritis like a chronic condition that requires ongoing management. Schedule recheck exams every 6 months to evaluate pain levels and adjust medications. Use a pain scoring system (like the Feline Musculoskeletal Pain Index or Canine Brief Pain Inventory) to track your pet's comfort at home. Keep a journal noting when your pet seems stiff or reluctant to move. Work with your vet to combine treatments--meds, supplements, diet, and physical therapy often work better together than alone. Don't forget mental enrichment: food puzzles, gentle nose work, and short training sessions keep your pet engaged without taxing joints. Many arthritic pets live many happy years with proper care. As your pet ages, be proactive about pain--don't wait until they are crying out. If you notice your pet withdrawing or becoming aggressive when touched, those are cries for help. With the right plan, your furry friend can enjoy quality time with you well into their golden years.
