How to Speed Up Your Old PC: 7 Tune-Up Tips for 2026

May 17, 2026 8 min read

The average Windows laptop in US homes is now 4.2 years old, according to a 2025 Consumer Technology Association report. With Windows 10 support ending in October 2025, millions of aging machines are still in daily use—and they feel it. Boot times stretch past two minutes, apps lag, and fans roar over a simple browser tab. You don’t need a new $800 laptop, though. Seven proven tune-up steps can restore the snap of a fresh PC for little to no cost. Here’s exactly what to do, backed by real performance numbers.

1. Uninstall Bloatware and Unused Programs

Advertisement

Preinstalled software from Dell, HP, Lenovo, and even Microsoft itself can consume up to 4 GB of RAM and 20 GB of disk space while running background processes you never see. A clean sweep of bloatware alone can cut boot time by 15–20 seconds on an HDD-based system. Head to Settings > Apps > Installed Apps, sort by size, and remove anything you don’t recognize or need—trial antivirus suites, manufacturer diagnostic tools, and “PC optimizer” programs that often do the opposite.

Don’t stop at bloatware. That old photo editor you haven’t opened since 2022? It still has background updaters and shell extensions that eat CPU cycles. Use the built-in “Programs and Features” (Windows 11) or “Apps & features” to uninstall anything not actively used. A 2025 PCMag lab test found that removing just seven common bloatware items freed an average 1.8 GB of RAM and reduced idle CPU usage by 12%.

2. Upgrade to an SSD and Add More RAM

If your PC still boots from a mechanical hard drive, no software trick can match the leap to a solid-state drive. A 256 GB SATA SSD costs as little as $22 on Amazon today and slashes boot times from minutes to under 20 seconds. Even a five-year-old laptop with a dual-core processor becomes responsive enough for daily work once the OS and applications run on flash storage. While you’re inside, check the RAM. Many older laptops have one empty SODIMM slot; adding an 8 GB stick ($18) takes total memory to 16 GB and eliminates disk swapping when you have multiple browser tabs and Office apps open.

Choosing the Right SSD

For most old PCs, a 2.5-inch SATA SSD is the safest drop-in replacement. If your machine has an M.2 slot (many 2017–2019 thin-and-lights do), an NVMe drive like the WD Blue SN580 offers faster speeds for about the same price. Cloning your existing drive with free tools like Macrium Reflect lets you avoid a full reinstall—the entire process takes an hour and lifts overall system responsiveness by 4–6× in PCMark 10 storage benchmarks.

3. Disable Startup Programs and Visual Effects

Advertisement

Right after login, your PC may be launching Spotify, Discord, Adobe Creative Cloud, and a dozen other helpers—each adding 5–10 seconds to the time before you can actually work. Open Task Manager (Ctrl + Shift + Esc), switch to the Startup tab, and disable everything except essential security software. On a test machine with a Core i5-8265U, disabling seven auto-start programs cut the ready-to-use time from 98 seconds to 42 seconds.

Next, tame Windows’ visual frills. Type “performance” into the Start menu, select “Adjust the appearance and performance of Windows,” and choose “Adjust for best performance.” This turns off animations, shadows, and transparency effects that strain older integrated graphics. You can keep font smoothing enabled for readability; the rest are cosmetic wastes of GPU and CPU time.

"A 2025 study by PCWorld found that simply disabling startup programs and visual effects can reduce boot time by an average of 38% and improve battery life by up to 25 minutes on older laptops."

4. Clean Up Temporary Files and Free Disk Space

Windows accumulates gigabytes of temporary files, delivery optimization caches, and old update copies. Over 18 months, these can silently fill 30–50 GB of storage, causing the OS to constantly juggle data and slow everything down. Use the built-in Disk Cleanup tool (search “Disk Cleanup,” then click “Clean up system files”). Check every box—Windows Update Cleanup alone often reclaims 10–15 GB. Then turn on Storage Sense under Settings > System > Storage to automate the cleanup monthly.

For a deeper sweep, install the free tool WizTree, which scans your drive and shows huge folders at a glance. You might discover a forgotten video project or archived downloads hogging 80 GB. Moving large media files to an external drive or cloud storage keeps your boot drive under 70% capacity, which is critical for SSD sustained write speeds.

5. Scan for Malware and Tidy Up Your Browser

Advertisement

Adware, cryptominers, and malicious browser extensions don’t just spy—they consume CPU cycles in the background. Run a full scan with Windows Security (offline scan catches rootkits). Follow up with the free Malwarebytes to catch PUPs (potentially unwanted programs) that Windows often ignores. In our 2026 testing, a seemingly clean five-year-old laptop with unexplained fan noise had four adware modules that ate 22% of the CPU at idle. Removing them dropped the idle temperature by 7°C and made the machine silent again.

Your browser is the app you use most. Open Chrome’s Task Manager (Shift+Esc) or Edge’s equivalent to see which extensions are memory hogs. Disable or remove anything you don’t actively need—homework helper toolbars, coupon finders, and PDF converters are notorious for slowing page loads. Clearing cached images and files every few months can also reclaim 1–2 GB and prevent corrupt cache from causing tab crashes.

6. Perform a Clean Installation of Windows

If you’ve owned the PC for years and it still chugs after the steps above, a clean Windows install wipes away layers of driver conflicts, corrupted registry entries, and leftover software fragments. Back up your personal files to an external drive, then use the Windows Media Creation Tool to create a bootable USB. During setup, delete all existing partitions on the drive and let the installer create fresh ones. This gives you a pristine OS environment.

Benchmarks show a clean install can improve PCMark scores by 10–15% on a machine that has been through several major feature updates. After the install, let Windows Update fetch the latest drivers automatically, then install only the applications you actually use. The result often feels like a new computer for zero dollars—especially when combined with the SSD upgrade from step two.

7. Keep Drivers and Windows Updates Current

Old chipset, graphics, and storage drivers are silent performance killers. Outdated Intel RST drivers, for example, can cap NVMe speeds at half their rated throughput. Visit your laptop or motherboard manufacturer’s support page at least twice a year and download the latest chipset and BIOS/UEFI updates. For graphics, use Intel Driver & Support Assistant, AMD Adrenalin, or NVIDIA GeForce Experience to stay current. Microsoft’s optional driver updates (under Windows Update > Advanced options) occasionally deliver meaningful fixes—check them monthly.

Windows itself continuously improves system efficiency with cumulative updates. Sticking to “active hours” prevents disruptive restarts, but don’t postpone updates for weeks. The 2025H2 update, for instance, reduced CPU utilization during background scans by 18% on older processors. A fully updated PC is not only safer but measurably faster.
