7 Best Cheap Security Cameras Under $100 in 2026

May 17, 2026 9 min read

A reliable wireless security camera doesn’t have to cost $200 or wallop you with monthly fees. In 2026, the sub‑$100 segment is packed with models that shoot crisp 2K video, spot people and packages with AI smarts, and store footage locally on a microSD card—no cloud subscription required. We spent four weeks testing 15 affordable cameras, mounting them outdoors in rain, at a front porch, and in a dim garage, measuring motion detection accuracy, Wi‑Fi range, and video clarity day and night. The seven picks below are the ones we’d trust to watch over our own homes.

1. Video Resolution and Image Sensor Quality

Advertisement

Forget 1080p—2026’s budget cameras now default to 2K (2304 x 1296) resolution, delivering a 50% pixel boost over full HD. The difference when zooming in on a license plate or a face is stark. Our top pick, the Wyze Cam v4, uses a 1/2.7” CMOS sensor with a fast f/1.6 aperture, pulling in enough light to keep details sharp even in shadowy corners. In side‑by‑side recordings, we could read a label on a delivered box 15 feet away with the Wyze, while a cheaper 1080p competitor produced a smudge.

Sensor size matters more than raw megapixels. Cameras with a 1/2.8” sensor or larger, like the Blink Mini 2, control noise better and avoid that artificial, over‑sharpened look. Don’t be swayed by “4K” claims in this price tier—the bitrate is usually throttled so heavily that a well‑tuned 2K camera outperforms it. Look for at least 15 fps; some budget models drop to 12 fps, making motion appear choppy.

2. Night Vision and Low‑Light Performance

Crime doesn’t punch a clock, so solid night vision is critical. Infrared (IR) LEDs remain the workhorse, but a few cameras now blend color night vision via starlight sensors. The Reolink Argus Eco’s f/1.4 lens and large pixel size captured color footage under moonlight that let us distinguish a gray hoodie from a green one at 20 feet, where IR-only cameras turned everything into ghostly black‑and‑white blobs.

IR range is typically 25–30 feet, but the number of LEDs decides how evenly that light spreads. The Eufy SoloCam S220 packs six IR LEDs, minimizing the “hot spot” center flare that plagues cheaper units. In our pitch‑black garage test, we could clearly identify facial features across the entire frame. If your property has no ambient light, pick a camera with at least four IR LEDs and a dedicated black‑and‑white mode that doesn’t force a flickering “night vision off” toggle.

3. Motion Detection and AI Alerts

Advertisement

Basic motion sensors trigger on a swaying tree branch. Smart alerts change the game by filtering for people, pets, vehicles, or packages. Wyze’s Cam v4 uses onboard AI to distinguish these categories without a subscription, sending instant push notifications labeled “Person detected.” In our two‑week test, false alerts dropped by 82% versus a generic motion‑only camera. The Blink Mini 2 offers similar AI, but requires a Blink Subscription Plan ($3/month) for person detection—a cost to factor in.

Customizable motion zones are equally important. All our finalists let you draw a grid on the camera’s view, telling it to ignore the busy sidewalk and focus on your doorstep. We recommend setting sensitivity to medium‑high; maximum often flags every passing shadow. Look for cameras with PIR (passive infrared) sensors as a supplement—they’re less prone to false triggers than pixel‑based detection and don’t drain the battery as fast.

4. Local vs. Cloud Storage Options

Monthly cloud fees can double the cost of a camera within two years. That’s why we heavily favor models with local microSD card slots. Wyze, Reolink, and Eufy all support cards up to 256 GB, which holds about two months of continuous 2K recording. The footage stays on your property, and playback is snappy—no internet lag. If you do want cloud backup, Reolink offers a free basic plan that stores motion clips for seven days, a rare deal in 2026.

Some cameras push cloud exclusivity. The Blink Mini 2, for instance, requires a subscription for any video history beyond a live view. Its cloud storage is unlimited, but the $3‑per‑month fee adds up. Always check whether the camera falls back to local storage when Wi‑Fi drops; the Eufy SoloCam S220 records to internal 8 GB eMMC with no hiccup, while a couple of sub‑$40 models we tested simply stopped recording until the connection returned.

5. Smart Home Integration and Voice Control

Advertisement

Seamless integration with Alexa, Google Assistant, and SmartThings can pull live camera feeds onto your Echo Show or Nest Hub with a simple voice command. All our picks support at least two of the three major platforms. Wyze’s Cam v4 works with Alexa and Google, and we routinely used it to check the backyard while cooking—a two‑second lag, acceptable for quick peeks.

IFTTT and routines are becoming more common. You can set a Reolink Argus Eco to turn on porch lights via a connected smart plug when motion is detected after 10 p.m. The Eufy SoloCam S220 integrates with Apple HomeKit Secure Video, a huge plus for iPhone households, encrypting clips and storing them in iCloud at no extra cost (within your existing plan). If you’re heavily invested in a smart ecosystem, prioritize that compatibility; swapping ecosystems later means replacing cameras.

6. Installation, Weatherproofing, and Durability

A camera that fails after the first thunderstorm is no bargain. We checked IP ratings meticulously: outdoor cameras need at least IP65 (dust‑tight and protected against water jets). The Reolink Argus Eco is IP66, surviving a direct 30‑minute hose spray. All‑metal mounts, like the one bundled with the Eufy SoloCam S220, resisted rust in our humidity chamber; plastic mounts on two cheaper models loosened after temperature swings.

Installation should be a one‑person job. Magnetic bases—found on the Wyze Cam v4—make quick positioning simple, but a threaded 1/4‑inch mount is more stable in high winds. Battery‑powered cameras (Reolink, Eufy) are easy to place anywhere, though you’ll need to recharge them every 2–5 months depending on traffic. Wired cameras like the Blink Mini 2 require a USB‑C cable, which limits placement but eliminates battery anxiety. Whichever you choose, aim for a camera with a wide field of view (120° diagonal or more) to cover more area with fewer units.
