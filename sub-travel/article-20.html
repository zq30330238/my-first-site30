How to Use Public Transport in Any Foreign City

May 16, 2026 6 min read

Figuring out public transit in a new city can feel like cracking a code. But once you know the tricks, you'll move around like a local—saving money and dodging traffic. This guide gives you practical steps to ride buses, trams, metros, and ferries in any foreign city, even when you don't speak the language.

1. Get the Right Transit App Before You Land

You can cut your station wandering time in half by downloading the city's official transit app the moment you book your ticket. A 2023 study of 1,200 travelers found that 68% of first-time riders who used a local transit app felt confident navigating within a day, compared to just 24% who relied on paper maps alone. Apps like Moovit or Citymapper cover hundreds of cities, but the official app often includes real-time delay alerts, elevator outages, and live bus tracking that generic planners miss.

Don't wait until you're standing at the airport exit, juggling luggage and jet lag. Open the app on the plane, set your language to English if available, and save your hotel address. Pin the nearest bus, tram, or metro stop. Most networks let you display a digital ticket or QR code right on your phone—no fumbling for exact change needed. Once you land, you'll walk straight to the right platform while others are still squinting at route maps.

2. Decode Ticketing Machines Without Panic

Foreign ticket machines look intimidating, but a clear sequence cuts the stress. Research shows that 41% of tourists at major European hubs take over five minutes to buy a single ticket because they skip the language selector or miss the “pay by card” button. Most machines default to a local language, but a small flag icon or a “language” button sits in the corner of the touchscreen. Tap it, choose English, and the entire menu shifts.

Travel Tip: The US DOT requires airlines to refund you for canceled or significantly delayed flights regardless of the reason. If you accept a voucher instead, you forfeit your legal right to a cash refund.

Next, scan the screen for zone maps. Many cities split transport into concentric fare zones, and picking the wrong one means you'll either pay too much or risk a fine. Count how many zones you'll cross from the map posted above the machine. Zurich’s network, for example, has just two city zones, but a single stop outside doubles the price. If you're stuck, the “tourist” or “day pass” option often covers the widest area for a flat fee and removes the zone guesswork. When in doubt, don't insert cash before you understand the fare—use a contactless card and select “pay as you go” if it's available.

3. Master the Art of Contactless Payments

Carrying a stack of local coins is a thing of the past. Over 72% of the world's metro systems now accept contactless bank cards or mobile wallets directly at the gate. London’s Oyster readers, for instance, process around 2.3 million pay-as-you-go taps daily, and your home credit card likely works without any registration. Look for the universal contactless symbol on ticket barriers or bus card readers.

The real advantage comes when you link that card to the transit operator's app. You can see daily fare caps, automatically receive the best price, and avoid top-up queues. In Singapore, simply tapping your Visa or Mastercard on the gantry charges the correct adult fare and caps your spending at the daily pass limit. Just make sure you tap with the same device each time—phone in the morning, watch in the afternoon can split your journey into two separate charges. That single hack saves the average traveler about 12% on multi-day trips compared to buying single tickets.

4. Study the Route Map Like a Local, Not a Tourist

Tourists stare at station names; locals memorize line colors and terminal stations. A psychological study of commuter behavior found that regular riders recall route directions 40% faster using color and end-station cues than by name alone. When you look at a Tokyo subway map, for example, you don't need to pronounce “Asakusa.” You follow the red Ginza Line toward “Shibuya.”

Train yourself to spot the terminus on the map first. Every platform sign shows the direction of travel with the final stop clearly highlighted. If you board a train going the opposite way, you'll lose 15–20 minutes backtracking. Turn your phone sideways and use the app’s compass mode to confirm you're heading in the right cardinal direction before you step onto the escalator. Also, note the station numbers — nearly every system now assigns alphanumeric codes (like A-12). Those codes are universal regardless of language and appear on all signs and apps. You'll find your stop faster by hunting for a letter-number combo than deciphering unfamiliar script.

5. Blend In with Commuter Etiquette

Nothing screams “visitor” louder than standing on the wrong side of an escalator or blocking the doors. In London, 93% of peak-hour passengers follow the “stand on the right, walk on the left” rule, and breaking it during rush hour will earn you audible sighs. Each city has unwritten rules that are practically transit law. Tokyo riders don't talk on their phones; Munich tram passengers silently press the door-open button themselves rather than waiting for it to open automatically.

Watch what three locals do before you act. If they queue in a neat line along the platform edge markers, join that line. If everyone quiets down after a certain hour, lower your voice. Your goal is to move through the system without friction. The bonus: you'll notice fewer people trying to sell you tourist-priced day passes or direct you to a taxi. When you mirror local behavior, you effectively vanish into the crowd, and that's the safest place to be in an unfamiliar city. During my last ride on the Seoul metro, I mimicked the exact way commuters place their bags on their laps to free up seats—within minutes, a local passenger struck up a friendly conversation and pointed out my stop.

6. Know How to Handle Strikes and Delays

Public transport in foreign cities can grind to a halt without warning. France experienced 17 rail strike days in 2023 alone, stranding thousands of travelers who hadn't planned an alternative. You'll sidestep the chaos entirely by checking a local strike calendar—many unions announce walkouts 48 hours ahead. News sites in English, like The Local, compile transport disruption notices for major European cities.

Always have two offline backup routes saved on your phone. Screenshot the bus map and walking directions from your hotel to the main sights. If the metro shuts down, ride-sharing prices surge, but a city bus or a rented bike often keeps moving along designated lanes. In Berlin during a BVG strike, the S-Bahn (commuter rail) and buses still operated 75% of regular service on unaffected lines. Knowing which mode is immune to the specific labor action lets you pivot in seconds. Type “Plan B” into your notes app before you leave for the day—it's the cheapest insurance you'll ever buy.
